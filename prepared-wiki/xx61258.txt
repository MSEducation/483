[tpl]transl|ar|ALA|Abu Yūsuf Yaʻqūb ibn ʼIsḥāq aṣ-Ṣabbāḥ al-Kindī[/tpl] ([tpl]lang-ar|أبو يوسف يعقوب بن إسحاق الصبّاح الكندي[/tpl], [tpl]lang-la|Alkindus[/tpl]) (c. 801–873 CE), known as "the Philosopher of the Arabs", was an Iraqi Muslim Arab philosopher, mathematician, physician, and musician. Al-Kindi was a descendant of the Kinda tribe. He was born and educated in Basra,http://plato.stanford.edu/entries/al-kindi/ before going to pursue further studies in Baghdad. Al-Kindi became a prominent figure in the House of Wisdom, and a number of Abbasid Caliphs appointed him to oversee the translation of Greek scientific and philosophical texts into the Arabic language. This contact with "the philosophy of the ancients" (as Greek philosophy was often referred to by Muslim scholars) had a profound effect on his intellectual development, and led him to write hundreds of original treatises of his own on a range of subjects ranging from metaphysics, ethics, logic and psychology, to medicine, pharmacology,[tpl]cite book|last=Corbin|first=Henry|title=History of Islamic philosophy|year=1993|publisher=Kegan Paul International|isbn=0-7103-0416-1|page=155[/tpl] mathematics, astronomy, astrology and optics, and further afield to more practical topics like perfumes, swords, jewels, glass, dyes, zoology, tides, mirrors, meteorology and earthquakes.[tpl]cite book|last=Adamson|first=Peter|title=The Cambridge companion to Arabic philosophy|year=2006|publisher=Cambridge University Press|isbn=978-0-521-52069-0|editor-last=Adamson|editor-first=Peter|editor2-last=Taylor|editor2-first=R.|chapter=Al-Kindı¯ and the reception of Greek philosophy|page=33[/tpl]Adamson, p7
In the field of mathematics, al-Kindi played an important role in introducing Indian numerals to the Islamic and Christian world.[ref]
