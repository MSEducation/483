Field Marshal Sir Charles Comyn Egerton, GCB, DSO (10 November 1848 – 20 February 1921) was a senior Indian Army officer. Early in his career he took part in the Second Anglo-Afghan War, the Hazara Expedition and operations in the Khyber Pass. He went on to command the Bannu column during operations in Waziristan in 1894 and to serve as senior staff officer for the Tochi Expedition in 1897. Egerton took command of the troops in British Somaliland fighting the Somalis under Mohammed Abdullah Hassan in June 1903; he had a significant success at Jidbali in January 1904 killing 1,000 of Hassan's men. His last appointment was as General Officer Commanding Secunderabad District in 1904 before retiring in 1907.

==Military career==

Born the third son of Major-General Caledon Egerton and Margaret Egerton (née Cumming) and educated at Rossall School and the Royal Military College Sandhurst, Egerton was commissioned into the 31st Regiment of Foot on 9 June 1867.[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=23260|supp=|startpage=3229|date=7 June 1867|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] He immediately transferred to the 76th Regiment of Foot in India.Heathcote, p. 115 Promoted to lieutenant on 19 October 1869,[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=23939|supp=|startpage=219|date=17 January 1873|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] he joined the Indian Staff Corps on 11 December 1872[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=23927|supp=|startpage=6254|date=10 December 1872|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] before being promoted to captain on 8 June 1879[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=24761|supp=|startpage=5456|date=12 September 1879|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] on appointment to a squadron in the 3rd Punjab Cavalry.
Egerton served with his regiment in the Second Anglo-Afghan War in 1879 and was mentioned in despatches.[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=24909|supp=|startpage=6538|date=3 December 1880|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] He became Assistant Adjutant-General of the Punjab Frontier Force on 30 November 1886[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=25811|supp=|startpage=2399|date=27 April 1888|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] and, having been promoted to major on 8 June 1887,[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=25727|supp=|startpage=4243|date=5 August 1887|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] he took part in the Hazara Expedition in 1888 and in operations in the Khyber Pass in 1891 for which he was awarded the Distinguished Service Order (DSO) on 30 May 1891.[tpl]cite web|url=http://www.oxforddnb.com/view/article/32986|title=Sir Charles Egerton|publisher=Oxford Dictionary of National Biography|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] Promoted to lieutenant colonel on 1 September 1891,[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=26226|supp=|startpage=6229|date=24 November 1891|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] he became Second-in-Command of his regiment in 1892 and commanded the Bannu column during operations in Waziristan in 1894[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=26639|supp=|startpage=3737|date=2 July 1895|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] before becoming Assistant Quartermaster-General at Bengal Command on 21 June 1895.[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=26636|supp=|startpage=3528|date=21 June 1895|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] He was appointed Companion of the Order of the Bath (CB) and then promoted to colonel on 1 September 1895.[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=26667|supp=|startpage=5407|date=1 October 1895|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl]
Egerton commanded a contingent of troops sent to Suakin in 1896 to hold the town when its permanent garrison was withdrawn for service under Lord Kitchener following which he became an aide-de-camp to the Queen on 17 November 1896.[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=26795|supp=|startpage=6272|date=17 November 1896|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] He was senior staff officer and Commander of the 1st Brigade for the Tochi Expedition in 1897 before becoming Commander-in-Chief Punjab Command with local promotion to major-general on 1 April 1899.[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=27095|supp=|startpage=4141|date=4 July 1899|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] He was promoted to the local rank of lieutenant general on 3 April 1900,[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=27205|supp=|startpage=3969|date=26 June 1900|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] and reverted to the Frontier Force in October 1901 when his command in Punjab ended.[tpl]Cite newspaper The Times |articlename=Naval & Military intelligence|day_of_week=Monday |date=14 October 1901 |page_number=10 |issue=36585| [/tpl]  A promotion to the substantive rank of major-general followed on 1 April 1902[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=27546|supp=|startpage=2618|date=24 April 1903|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] and, after leading an expedition to Waziristan later that year, he was advanced to Knight Commander of the Order of the Bath (KCB) on 1 January 1903.[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=27511|supp=yes|startpage=4|date=30 December 1902|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl]
Egerton took command of the troops in British Somaliland fighting the Somalis under Mohammed Abdullah Hassan on 27 June 1903.[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=27601|supp=|startpage=5967|date=29 September 1903|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] Promoted to the substantive rank of lieutenant general on 28 October 1903,[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=27666|supp=yes|startpage=2302|date=12 April 1904|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] he became Commander-in-Chief Madras Command.Whitaker's Almanack 1905, p. 484 He had a significant success at Jidbali in January 1904 killing 1,000 of Hassan's men.[tpl]cite web|url=http://query.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=F20E10FC39541B728DDDAB0994D9405B848CF1D3|title=1,000 Dervishes slain; British Rout the "Mad" Mullah's Forces in Somaliland|publisher=New York Times|date=12 January 1904|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] However in March 1904 questions were still being asked in the British Parliament as to whether Hassan (then known as the "Mad Mullah") understood that operations would only end when he was captured or killed.[tpl]cite web|url=http://hansard.millbanksystems.com/commons/1904/mar/28/the-mad-mullah|title=The Mad Mullah|date=28 March 1904|publisher=Hansard|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] 
Egerton became colonel of the 23rd Punjab Cavalry (Frontier Force) in 1904.Heathcote, p. 116 He was advanced to Knight Grand Cross of the Order of the Bath (GCB) on 24 June 1904[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=27688|supp=yes|startpage=4007|date=21 June 1904|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] and promoted to full general on 28 October 1906. After retiring on 5 February 1907,[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=28005|supp=|startpage=1929|date=19 March 1907|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] he became a member of the Council of India from which post he stood down in 1917. In recognition of his long service he was promoted to field marshal on 16 March 1917.[tpl]LondonGazette|issue=29986|supp=|startpage=2634|date=16 March 1917|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl]
In retirement Egerton wrote about his wartime experiences and was the author of a book entitled "Hill Warfare on the North-West frontier of India".[tpl]cite web|url=http://www.amazon.co.uk/dp/B000X7G7IE |title=Hill Warfare on the North-West frontier of India|publisher=Amazon books|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl] He died on 20 February 1921 and was buried at St Mark's Churchyard at Highcliffe in Dorset.[tpl]cite web|url=http://www.findagrave.com/cgi-bin/fg.cgi?page=gr&GRid=39600497|title=Sir Charles Comyn Egerton|publisher=Find a Grave|accessdate=22 June 2013[/tpl]

==Family==

In 1877 Egerton married Anna Wellwood and they went on to have three sons.
