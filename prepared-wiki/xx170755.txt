[[Bernard Campbell Fletcher]]

Bernard Campbell Fletcher DSO, MC (1898 â€“ 1968) was an officer in the British Army during the First World War and the Second World War.

==Career==

Fletcher was commissioned from the Royal Military College, Sandhurst as a Second Lieutenant into the Highland Light Infantry in 1916.[tpl]Londongazette| issue=29537| date=6 April 1916| startpage=3685| supp=y| accessdate=1 August 2012[/tpl] During the First World War he won the Military Cross (gazetted in March 1919)[tpl]LondonGazette| issue=31219| date=9 March 1919| startpage=3240| supp=y| accessdate=2 August 2012[/tpl] and was gassed with mustard gas. The citation for his MC, published in the London Gazette in October 1919 read:[tpl]quote|Lt. (A./Capt.) Bernard Campbell Fletcher, High. L.I., attd. 15th Bn. On the 29th September, 1918, in the attack on Le Tronquoy, he led his company through the village, gaining all of his objectives and capturing about ten machine guns and 60 prisoners. He then, reorganised his company himself, having lost all his platoon commanders. Later, his fine example was largely responsible for beating off two enemy counter-attacks, he firing a Lewis gun himself. Throughout he showed most marked courage and did splendid work.[tpl]LondonGazette| issue=31583| date=3 October 1919| startpage=12290| supp=y| accessdate=2 August 2012[/tpl][/tpl] After the war he spent periods in North Russia, Egypt, Palestine and India during which he learned to speak Arabic and Hindustani. In 1929 he attended Staff College[tpl]LondonGazette| issue=33459| date=22 January 1929| startpage=544| accessdate=2 August 2012[/tpl] after which he returned to his regiment in India. Between 1933 and 1937 he held an appointment as Staff Captain in India[tpl]LondonGazette| issue=33963| date=25 July 1933| startpage=4964| accessdate=2 August 2012[/tpl][tpl]LondonGazette| issue=34432| date=3 September 1937| startpage=5563| accessdate=2 August 2012[/tpl] and was promoted to Major in 1935.[tpl]LondonGazette| issue=34204| date=4 October 1935| startpage=6217| accessdate=2 August 2012[/tpl] In 1938 he was given command of the 2nd battalion Highland Light Infantry[tpl]sfn|Brett-James|1951| loc=Chapter VI[/tpl] and his promotion to Lieutenant-Colonel was gazetted in 1939.[tpl]LondonGazette| issue=34641| date=30 June 1939| startpage=4438| accessdate=2 August 2012[/tpl]
In World War II Fletcher and his battalion took part in the East African Campaign as an element of Indian 10th Infantry Brigade which formed part of Indian 5th Infantry Division. When the brigade's commander, Bill Slim, was wounded on 21 January 1941 Fletcher was given temporary command until the arrival of a permanent replacement on 20 March.[tpl]sfn|Brett-James|1951|loc=Chapter V[/tpl] Reverting to his battalion command, Fletcher was subsequently given command of Fletcher Force,[tpl]sfn|Brett-James|1951|loc=Chapter V[/tpl] an ad hoc grouping of armour and mobile infantry created to advance into Keren at the end of the Battle of Keren and to exploit towards Asmara. He was then appointed to command Flitforce (a reference to his nickname),[tpl]sfn|Brett-James|1951|loc=Chapter VI[/tpl] an ad hoc grouping created to pursue the retreating Italians towards Adigrat. On 13 April, Fletcher was promoted to command the Indian 9th Infantry Brigade, also part of Indian 5th Infantry Division. For his service during the East African campaign Fletcher was awarded the DSO[tpl]LondonGazette |issue=35396 |date=26 December 1941 |startpage=7333 |endpage= |supp=y |accessdate=23 June 2008[/tpl] and was mentioned in despatches.[tpl]LondonGazette |issue=35396 |date=26 December 1941 |startpage=7348|endpage= |supp=y |accessdate=2 August 2012[/tpl]
Fletcher retired from the army on 3 September 1948 a Lieutenant-Colonel and was granted the honorary rank of brigadier.[tpl]LondonGazette |issue=38394|date=31 August 1948 |startpage=4808 |endpage= |supp=y |accessdate=23 June 2008[/tpl]
