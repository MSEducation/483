[[Create, read, update and delete]]

[tpl]Redirect|CRUD|other uses|Crud (disambiguation)[tpl]![/tpl]Crud[/tpl]
In computer programming, create, read, update and delete (CRUD) (Sometimes called SCRUD with an "S" for Search) are the four basic functions of persistent storage.[tpl]cite web |url=http://www.infoworld.com/d/developer-world/rest-and-crud-impedance-mismatch-927 |publisher=InfoWorld  |work=Developer World |title=REST and CRUD: the Impedance Mismatch |date=29 January 2007 |first=Martin |last=Heller[/tpl] Sometimes CRUD is expanded with the words retrieve instead of read, modify instead of update, or destroy instead of delete. It is also sometimes used to describe user interface conventions that facilitate viewing, searching, and changing information; often using computer-based forms and reports. The term was likely first popularized by James Martin in his 1983 book Managing the Data-base Environment.[tpl]Google books|ymy4AAAAIAAJ|Managing the Data-base Environment|page=381[/tpl] The acronym may be extended to CRUDL to cover listing of large data sets which bring additional complexity such as pagination when the data sets are too large to hold easily in memory.
Another variation of CRUD is BREAD, an acronym for "Browse, Read, Edit, Add, Delete".
