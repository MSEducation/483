[tpl]transl|sem|示l[/tpl] (written aleph-lamed, e.g. [tpl]lang-uga|[/tpl], [tpl]lang-phn|[/tpl], [tpl]lang-syc|[/tpl], [tpl]lang-he|[/tpl], [tpl]lang-ar|廿[/tpl] or [tpl]lang|ar|廿[/tpl], cognate to [tpl]lang-akk|ilu[/tpl]) is a Northwest Semitic word meaning "deity".
In the Canaanite religion, or Levantine religion as a whole, El or Il was a god also known as the Father of humanity and all creatures, and the husband of the goddess Asherah as recorded in the clay tablets of Ugarit (modern [tpl]transl|apc|Ras Shamr[/tpl][tpl]lang-ar|乇兀爻 卮乇丕[/tpl], Syria).[tpl]sfn|Matthews|2004|p=79[/tpl]
The bull was symbolic to El and his son Ba驶al Hadad, and they both wore bull horns on their headdress.[tpl]sfn|van der Toorn|1999|p=181[/tpl] He may have been a desert god at some point, as the myths say that he had two wives and built a sanctuary with them and his new children in the desert. El had fathered many gods, but most important were Hadad, Yam, and Mot.

==Linguistic forms and meanings==

Cognate forms are found throughout the Semitic languages. They include Ugaritic [tpl]lang|uga-Latn|示il[/tpl], pl. [tpl]lang|uga-Latn|示lm[/tpl];
Phoenician [tpl]lang|phn-Latn|示l[/tpl] pl. [tpl]lang|phn-Latn|示lm[/tpl];
Hebrew [tpl]lang|he-Latn|示l[/tpl], pl. [tpl]lang|he-Latn|示l卯m[/tpl];
Aramaic [tpl]lang|arc-Latn|示l[/tpl];
Akkadian [tpl]lang|akk-Latn|ilu[/tpl], pl. [tpl]lang|akk-Latn|ilnu[/tpl].
In northwest Semitic use, El was both a generic word for any god and the special name or title of a particular god who was distinguished from other gods as being "the god".[tpl]sfn|Smith|2001|p=135[/tpl]
El is listed at the head of many pantheons. El is the Father God among the Canaanites.
However, because the word sometimes refers to a god other than the great god l, it is frequently ambiguous as to whether l followed by another name means the great god l with a particular epithet applied or refers to another god entirely. For example, in the Ugaritic texts, 示il mlk is understood to mean "l the King" but 示il hd as "the god Hadad".
The Semitic root 示lh (Arabic [tpl]Transl|ar|DIN|示ilh[/tpl], Aramaic 示Alh, 示Elh, Hebrew 示elah) may be 示l with a parasitic h, and 示l may be an abbreviated form of 示lh.  In Ugaritic the plural form meaning "Gods" is [tpl]lang|uga-Latn|示ilhm[/tpl], equivalent to Hebrew [tpl]lang|he-Latn|示elh卯m[/tpl] "Gods". But in Hebrew this word is also regularly used for semantically singular "god" or "God".
The stem 示l is found prominently in the earliest strata of east Semitic, northwest Semitic, and south Semitic groups. Personal names including the stem 示l are found with similar patterns in both Amorite and South Arabic which indicates that probably already in Proto-Semitic 示l was both a generic term for "god" and the common name or title of a single particular god.
