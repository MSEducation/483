[[Charterhouse, Somerset]]

CATEGORIES: Mendip Hills, Villages in Mendip, Scheduled Ancient Monuments in Somerset

Charterhouse, also known as Charterhouse-on-Mendip, is a hamlet in the Mendip Hills Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty (AONB) in the English county of Somerset. The area between Charterhouse and Cheddar Gorge including Velvet Bottom and Ubley Warren is covered by the Cheddar Complex Site of Special Scientific Interest.

==Name==

The name is believed to come from the Carthusian order of Chartreuse in France, which was established in Witham (near Frome) in 1181 and formed a cell at Charterhouse in 1283 with a grant to mine lead ore.[tpl]cite book |last=Robinson |first=Stephen |authorlink= |coauthors= |title=Somerset Place Names |year=1992 |publisher=The Dovecote Press Ltd |location=Wimborne, Dorset |isbn=1-874336-03-2[/tpl][tpl]cite book |last=Reid |first= Robert Douglas |authorlink= |coauthors= |title=Some buildings of Mendip |year=1979 |publisher=The Mendip Society |location= |isbn=0-905459-16-4 [/tpl][tpl]cite web |url=http://www.mendiphillsaonb.org.uk/publications/up_081015_autumn07_lo.pdf |title=Autumn newsletter 2007 |accessdate=25 September 2007 |format=PDF |work=Mendip Hills AONB [/tpl]

==History==

There is evidence, in the form of burials in local caves, of human occupation since the late Neolithic/Early Bronze Age.[tpl]cite web | title=Mendip Hills: An Archaeological Survey of the Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty | work=Somerset County Council Archaeological Projects | url=http://www1.somerset.gov.uk/archives/hes/downloads/HES_MendipAONB.pdf | accessdate=15 January 2011[/tpl]
The lead and silver mines at Charterhouse, were first operated on a large scale by the Romans, from at least AD 49.[tpl]cite book|last=Havinden|first=Michael|title=The Somerset Landscape|publisher=Hodder and Stoughton|location=London|series=The making of the English landscape|pages=58–59|isbn=0-340-20116-9|year=1981[/tpl] At first the lead/silver industries were tightly controlled by the Roman military, but within a short time the extraction of these metals was contracted out to civilian companies, probably because the silver content of the local ore was not particularly high.[tpl]cite web | title=Major Romano-British Settlement Charterhouse on Mendip, Avon | work=Roman Britain.org | url=http://www.roman-britain.org/places/charterhouse.htm | accessdate=28 October 2006[/tpl] There was also some kind of 'fortlet' here in the 1st century, and an amphitheatre.[tpl]cite web | title=Charterhouse | work=Big Roman Dig | url=http://www.channel4.com/history/microsites/B/bigromandig/database/2_31.jsp?activityId=361 | accessdate=28 October 2006[/tpl] The Roman landscape has been  designated as a Scheduled Ancient Monument.[tpl]cite book |last=Firth |first=Hannah |authorlink= |coauthors= |title=Mendip from the air |year=2007 |publisher=Somerset County Council |location=Taunton |isbn=978-0-86183-390-0 [/tpl]
The parish was part of the Winterstoke Hundred.[tpl]cite web|title=Somerset Hundreds|url=http://www.genuki.org.uk/big/eng/SOM/Miscellaneous/|publisher=GENUKI|accessdate=9 September 2011[/tpl]
After the dissolution of the monasteries, it was granted to Robert May who constructed a substantial house here and one of his descendants John May became High Sheriff of Somerset in 1602.
There is further evidence of mine workings in the medieval and Victorian periods,[tpl]cite web | title=Mendip Hills: An Archaeological Survey of the Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty | work=Somerset County Council Archaeological Projects | url=http://www.somerset.gov.uk/media/896B4/MendipAONB.pdf | accessdate=28 October 2006[/tpl][tpl]cite book |last=Gough |first=J.W. |authorlink= |coauthors= |title=The mines of Mendip |year=1967 |publisher=David & Charles |location=Newton Abbot, Devon[/tpl] some of which survives within the Blackmoor Nature Reserve owned by Somerset County Council. There is also evidence of a rectangular medieval enclosure.[tpl]cite book |last=Adkins |first=Lesley and Roy |authorlink= |coauthors= |title=A Field Guide to Somerset Archaeology |year=1992 |publisher=Dovecote Press |location=Wimborne, Dorset |isbn=0-946159-94-7 |pages=37–39[/tpl]

==AONB Centre==

The outdoor activity centre and headquarters of the Mendip Hills AONB is based at Charterhouse, with accommodation, classrooms and offices.

==Caves==

There are several caves in the limestone around the village including Manor Farm Swallet and Upper Flood Swallet.

==Church==

The Church of England parish church of St Hugh was built in 1908 by W.D. Caroe, on the initiative of the Rev. Menzies Lambrick,[tpl]cite journal |quotes= |last=Staveacre |first=Tony |authorlink= |coauthors= |date=December 2006 |title=Christmas at Charterhouse |journal=Mendip Times |volume=2 |issue=7 |pages=8 |id= |url= |accessdate=30 November 2006 [/tpl] from the former welfare hall for the lead miners. It is a Grade II* listed building.[tpl]cite web | title=Church of St Hugh | work=Images of England | url=http://www.imagesofengland.org.uk/details/default.aspx?id=268094 | accessdate=29 October 2006[/tpl] A cross in the churchyard[tpl]cite web | title=Churchyard Cross in churchyard, Church of St Hugh | work=Images of England | url=http://www.imagesofengland.org.uk/details/default.aspx?id=268095 | accessdate=29 October 2006[/tpl] and the churchyard wall[tpl]cite web | title=Churchyard Wall to Church of St Hugh | work=Images of England | url=http://www.imagesofengland.org.uk/details/default.aspx?id=268096 | accessdate=29 October 2006[/tpl] are also listed buildings.
The roof-truss, screen, rood, and altar are all made of carved whitened oak.
