[[Arabic diacritics]]

CATEGORIES: Arabic diacritics, Arabic words and phrases, Quranic orthography, Phonetic guides

The Arabic script has numerous diacritics, including i'jam [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|إِعْجَام[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|i‘jām[/tpl], consonant pointing), and tashkil [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|تَشْكِيل[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|tashkīl[/tpl], supplementary diacritics). The latter include the [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḥarakāt[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|حَرَكَات[/tpl][/tpl] (vowel marks; singular: [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḥarakah[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|حَرَكَة[/tpl][/tpl]). 
The Arabic script is an impure abjad, where short consonants and long vowels are represented by letters but short vowels and consonant length are not generally indicated in writing. [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|Tashkīl[/tpl] is optional to represent missing vowels and consonant length. Modern Arabic is nearly always written with consonant pointing, but occasionally unpointed texts are still seen. Early texts such as the Qur'an were initially written without pointing, and pointing was added later to determine the expected readings and interpretations.

==Tashkil (marks used as phonetic guides)==

The literal meaning of [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|tashkīl[/tpl] is 'forming'. As the normal Arabic text does not provide enough information about the correct pronunciation, the main purpose of [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|tashkīl[/tpl] (and [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḥarakāt[/tpl]) is to provide a phonetic guide or a phonetic aid; i.e. show the correct pronunciation. It serves the same purpose as furigana (also called "ruby") in Japanese or pinyin or zhuyin in Mandarin Chinese for children who are learning to read or foreign learners.
The bulk of Arabic script is written without [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḥarakāt[/tpl] (or short vowels). However, they are commonly used in some religious texts that demand strict adherence to pronunciation rules such as Qur'an [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|الْقُرْآن[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|al-Qur’ān[/tpl]). It is not uncommon to add [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḥarakāt[/tpl] to hadiths [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|الْحَدِيث[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|al-ḥadīth[/tpl]; plural: [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|aḥādīth[/tpl]) as well. Another use is in children's literature. Harakat are also used in ordinary texts when an ambiguity of pronunciation might arise. Vowelled Arabic dictionaries provide information about the correct pronunciation to both native and foreign Arabic speakers.
Short vowels can be included in cases where readers could not easily resolve word ambiguity from context alone, or simply wherever their writing might be considered aesthetically pleasing.

Some Arabic textbooks for foreigners now use [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḥarakāt[/tpl] as a phonetic guide to make learning reading Arabic easier. The other method used in textbooks is phonetic romanisation of unvocalised texts. Fully vocalised Arabic texts (i.e. Arabic texts with [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḥarakāt[/tpl]/diacritics) are sought after by learners of Arabic. Some online bilingual dictionaries also provide [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḥarakāt[/tpl] as a phonetic guide similarly to English dictionaries providing transcription.

===Harakat (short vowel marks)===

The [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḥarakāt[/tpl], which literally means 'motions', are the short vowel marks. There is some ambiguity as to which [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|tashkīl[/tpl] are also [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḥarakāt[/tpl]; the [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|tanwīn[/tpl], for example, are markers for both vowels and consonants.

====Fatḥah====

 [tpl]lang|ar|ـَ[/tpl] 
The [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|fatḥah[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|فَتْحَة[/tpl][/tpl] is a small diagonal line placed above a letter, and represents a short [tpl]IPA|/a/[/tpl]. The word [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|fatḥah[/tpl] itself ([tpl]lang|ar|فَتْحَة[/tpl]) means opening, and refers to the opening of the mouth when producing an [tpl]IPA|/a/[/tpl]. Example with [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|dāl[/tpl] (henceforth, the base consonant in the following examples): [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|دَ[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/da/[/tpl].
When a  [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|fatḥah[/tpl] is placed before the letter [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ا[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl]), it represents a long [tpl]IPA|/aː/[/tpl] (as in the English word "father"). Example: [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|دَا[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/daː/[/tpl]. The [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|fatḥah[/tpl] is not usually written in such cases.

====Kasrah====

 [tpl]lang|ar|ـِ[/tpl] 
A similar diagonal line below a letter is called a [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|kasrah[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|كَسْرَة[/tpl][/tpl] and designates a short [tpl]IPA|/i/[/tpl]. Example: [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|دِ[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/di/[/tpl].
When a [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|kasrah[/tpl] is placed before the letter [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ﻱ[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|yā’[/tpl]), it represents a long [tpl]IPA|/iː/[/tpl] (as in the English word "steed"). Example: [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|دِي[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/diː/[/tpl]. The [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|kasrah[/tpl] is usually not written in such cases but if [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|yā’[/tpl]  is pronounced as a diphthong [tpl]IPA|/aj/[/tpl], [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|fatḥah[/tpl] should be written on the preceding consonant to avoid mispronunciation. The word [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|kasrah[/tpl] means 'breaking'.

====Ḍammah====

 [tpl]lang|ar|ـُ[/tpl] 
The [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḍammah[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ضَمَّة[/tpl][/tpl] is a small curl-like diacritic placed above a letter to represent a short [tpl]IPA|/u/[/tpl]. Example: [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|دُ[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/du/[/tpl].
When a [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḍammah[/tpl] is placed before the letter [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|و[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|wāw[/tpl]), it represents a long [tpl]IPA|/uː/[/tpl] (as in the English word "blue"). Example: [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|دُو[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/duː/[/tpl]. The [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḍammah[/tpl] is usually not written in such cases but if [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|wāw[/tpl] is pronounced as a diphthong [tpl]IPA|/aw/[/tpl], [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|fatḥah[/tpl] should be written on the preceding consonant to avoid mispronunciation.

====Maddah====

 [tpl]lang|ar|ـٓ[/tpl] 
The [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|maddah[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|مَدَّة[/tpl][/tpl] is a tilde-like diacritic which can appear only on top of an [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl] and indicates a glottal stop [tpl]IPA|/ʔ/[/tpl] followed by a long [tpl]IPA|/aː/[/tpl].
In theory, the same sequence [tpl]IPA|/ʔaː/[/tpl] could also be represented by two [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl]s, as in *[tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|أَا[/tpl][/tpl], where a hamza above the first [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl] represents the [tpl]IPA|/ʔ/[/tpl] while the second [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl] represents the [tpl]IPA|/aː/[/tpl].  However, consecutive [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl]s are never used in the Arabic orthography.  Instead, this sequence must always be written as a single [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl] with a [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|maddah[/tpl] above it—the combination known as an [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif maddah[/tpl].  Example: [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|قُرْآن[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/qurˈʔaːn/[/tpl].

====Dagger alif====

 [tpl]lang|ar|ــٰ[/tpl] 
The superscript (or dagger) ''[tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|أَلِف خَنْجَرِيَّة[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif khanjarīyah[/tpl]), is written as short vertical stroke on top of a consonant. It indicates a long [tpl]IPA|/aː/[/tpl] sound where [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl] is normally not written, e.g. [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|هٰذَا[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|hādhā[/tpl]) or [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|رَحْمٰن[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|raḥmān[/tpl]'').
The dagger [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl] occurs in only a few words, but these include some common ones; it is seldom written, however, even in fully vocalised texts. Most keyboards do not have dagger [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl]. The word Allah [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|الله[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|Allāh[/tpl]) is usually produced automatically by entering [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif lām lām hāʾ[/tpl]. The word consists of [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl] + ligature of doubled [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|lām[/tpl] with a [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|shaddah[/tpl] and a dagger [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl] above [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|lām[/tpl].

====Alif waslah====

 [tpl]lang|ar|ٱ[/tpl] 
The [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|waṣlah[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|وَصْلَة[/tpl][/tpl], [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif waṣlah[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|أَلِف وَصْلَة[/tpl][/tpl] or [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|hamzat waṣl[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|هَمْزَة وَصْل[/tpl][/tpl] looks like a small letter [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ṣād[/tpl] on top of an [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ٱ[/tpl][/tpl] (also indicated by an [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ا[/tpl][/tpl] without a [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|hamzah[/tpl]). It means that the [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl] is not pronounced, e.g. [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|بٱسم[/tpl][/tpl].
It only occurs in the beginning of words (can occur after prepositions and the definite article). It is commonly found in imperative verbs, the perfective aspect of verb stems VII to X and their verbal nouns ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|maṣdar[/tpl]). The alif of the definite article is considered a [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|waṣlah[/tpl].
It occurs in phrases and sentences (connected speech, not isolated/dictionary forms) 1) to replace the elided hamza whose alif-seat has assimilated to the previous vowel, e.g., في ٱليمن or في اليمن (alif by itself is also indicative of the connecting hamza);
2) in hamza-initial imperative forms following a vowel, especially following the conjunction و, e.g., قُم و ٱشرب الماء

====Sukun====

 [tpl]lang|ar|ـْـ[/tpl] 
The [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|sukūn[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|سُكُون[/tpl][/tpl] is a circle-shaped diacritic placed above a letter. It indicates that the consonant to which it is attached is not followed by a vowel; this is a necessary symbol for writing consonant-vowel-consonant syllables, which are very common in Arabic. Example: [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|دَدْ[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|dad[/tpl].
The [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|sukūn[/tpl] may also be used to help represent a diphthong. A [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|fatḥah[/tpl] followed by the letter [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ﻱ[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|yā’[/tpl]) with a [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|sukūn[/tpl] over it indicates the diphthong [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ay[/tpl] (IPA [tpl]IPA|/aj/[/tpl]). A [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|fatḥah[/tpl] followed by the letter [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ﻭ[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|wāw[/tpl]) with a [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|sukūn[/tpl] indicates [tpl]IPA|/aw/[/tpl].

===Tanwin (final postnasalized or long vowels)===

 [tpl]lang|ar|ـٌ[/tpl]  [tpl]lang|ar|ـٍ[/tpl]  [tpl]lang|ar|ـً[/tpl] 
The three vowel diacritics may be doubled at the end of a word to indicate that the vowel is followed by the consonant n. These may or may not be considered [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḥarakāt[/tpl], and are known as [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|tanwīn[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|تَنْوِين[/tpl][/tpl], or nunation. The signs indicate, from right to left, [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|-un, -in, -an[/tpl].
These endings are used as non-pausal grammatical indefinite case endings in literary Arabic or classical Arabic (triptotes only). In a vocalised text, they may be written even if they are not pronounced (see pausa). See [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|i‘rāb[/tpl] for more details. In many spoken Arabic dialects, these endings are absent. Many Arabic textbooks introduce standard Arabic without these endings. The grammatical endings may not be written in some vocalized Arabic texts, as knowledge of [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|i‘rāb[/tpl] varies from country to country, and there is a trend in simplifying the Arabic grammar. 
The sign [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ـً[/tpl][/tpl] is most commonly written in combination with [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ـًا[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl]), [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ةً[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|tā’ marbūṭah[/tpl]) or stand-alone [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ءً[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|hamzah[/tpl]). [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|Alif[/tpl] should always be written (except for words ending in [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|tā’ marbūṭah, hamzah[/tpl] or diptotes), even if [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|an[/tpl] is not. ===Shaddah (consonant gemination mark)===

 [tpl]lang|ar|ـّـ[/tpl] 
The shadda or shaddah [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|شَدَّة[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|shaddah[/tpl]), or tashdid [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|تَشْدِيد[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|tashdīd[/tpl]), is a diacritic shaped like a small written Latin "w".
It is used to indicate gemination (consonant doubling or extra length), which is phonemic in Arabic. It is written above the consonant which is to be doubled. It is the only [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḥarakah[/tpl] that is sometimes used in ordinary spelling to avoid ambiguity. Example: [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|دّ[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/dd/[/tpl]; [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|madrasah[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|مَدْرَسَة[/tpl][/tpl] ('school') vs. [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|mudarrisah[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|مُدَرِّسَة[/tpl][/tpl] ('teacher', female).

==I'jam (phonetic distinctions of consonants)==

The ijam [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|إِعْجَام[/tpl][/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|i‘jām[/tpl]) are the pointing diacritics that distinguish various consonants that have the same form ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|rasm[/tpl]), such as [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ـبـ[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/b/[/tpl], [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ـتـ[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/t/[/tpl], [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ـثـ[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/θ/[/tpl], [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ـنـ[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/n/[/tpl], and [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ـيـ[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/j/[/tpl]. Typically ijam are not considered diacritics but part of the letter.
Early manuscripts of the [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|Qur’ān[/tpl] did not use diacritics either for vowels or to distinguish the different values of the [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|rasm[/tpl]. Vowel pointing was introduced first, as a red dot placed above, below, or beside the [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|rasm[/tpl], and later consonant pointing was introduced, as thin, short black single or multiple dashes placed above or below the rasm (image). These i‘jām became black dots about the same time as the [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|ḥarakāt[/tpl] became small black letters or strokes. 
Typically, Egyptians do not use dots under final [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|yā’[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ي[/tpl][/tpl], both in handwriting and in print, as [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif maqṣūrah[/tpl] [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|ى[/tpl][/tpl]. This practice is also used in copies of the [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|muṣḥaf[/tpl] (Qurʾān) scribed by [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|‘Uthman Ṭāhā[/tpl]. The same unification of [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|yā[/tpl] and [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif maqṣūrā[/tpl] has happened in Persian, resulting in what the Unicode Standard calls "[tpl]smallcaps|arabic letter farsi yeh[/tpl]", that looks exactly the same as [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|yā[/tpl] in initial and medial forms, but exactly the same as [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif maqṣūrah[/tpl] in final and isolated forms [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|fa|یـ  ـیـ  ـی[/tpl][/tpl].

==Hamza (glottal stop semi-consonant)==

 [tpl]lang|ar|ئ  ؤ  إ  أ[/tpl] 
Although often a diacritic is not considered a letter of the alphabet, the hamza [tpl]lang|ar|هَمْزَة[/tpl] ([tpl]transl|ar|ALA|hamzah[/tpl], glottal stop), often does stand as a separate letter in writing, is written in unpointed texts, and is not considered a [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|tashkīl[/tpl].  It may appear as a letter by itself or as a diacritic over or under an [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl], [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|wāw[/tpl], or [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|yā[/tpl].
Which letter is to be used to support the [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|hamzah[/tpl] depends on the quality of the adjacent vowels.
Consider the following words: [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|أَخ[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/ʔax/[/tpl] ("brother"), [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|إِسْرَائِيل[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/ʔisraːʔiːl/[/tpl] ("Israel"), [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|أُمّ[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/ʔumm/[/tpl] ("mother"). All three of above words "begin" with a vowel opening the syllable, and in each case, [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|alif[/tpl] is used to designate the initial glottal stop (the actual beginning). But if we consider middle syllables "beginning" with a vowel: [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|نَشْأَة[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/naʃʔa/[/tpl] ("origin"), [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|إِسْرَائِيل[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/ʔisraːʔiːl/[/tpl] ("Israel" — notice the [tpl]IPA|/ʔiːl/[/tpl] syllable), [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|رُؤُوس[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/ruʔuːs/[/tpl] ("heads", singular [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]lang|ar|رَأْس[/tpl][/tpl] [tpl]IPA|/raʔs/[/tpl]), the situation is different, as noted above. See the comprehensive article on [tpl]transl|ar|ALA|hamzah[/tpl] for more details.
