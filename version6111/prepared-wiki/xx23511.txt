[[Umami]]

CATEGORIES: Gustation, Flavour enhancers, Japanese words and phrases, Umami enhancers

Umami [tpl]IPAc-en|uː|ˈ|m|ɑː|m|i[/tpl], a savory taste,[tpl]cite web|url=http://www.telegraph.co.uk/foodanddrink/foodanddrinknews/7195114/Umami-in-a-tube-fifth-taste-goes-on-sale-in-supermarkets.html |date=9 February 2010 |title=Umami in a tube: 'fifth taste' goes on sale in supermarkets |publisher=The Daily Telegraph |author=Heidi Blake |accessdate=10 February 2011[/tpl][tpl]cite web |url=http://dictionary.cambridge.org/dictionary/british/umami |title=Cambridge Advanced Learner's Dictionary |publisher=Cambridge University Press |accessdate=1 January 2011[/tpl][tpl]cite web |url=http://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/umami |title=Merriam-Webster English Dictionary |publisher=Merriam-Webster, Incorporated |accessdate=1 January 2011[/tpl] is one of the five basic tastes (together with sweet, sour, bitter and salty).
A loanword from the Japanese [tpl]nihongo||うま味[/tpl], umami can be translated as "pleasant savory taste".[tpl]cite web |url=http://www.csse.monash.edu.au/~jwb/cgi-bin/wwwjdic.cgi?1C |title=EDICT's entry for umami |author=Jim Breen |accessdate=31 December 2010[/tpl] This particular writing was chosen by Professor Kikunae Ikeda  from umai (うまい) "delicious" and mi (味) "taste". The kanji 旨味 are used for a more general sense of a food as delicious.
People taste umami through receptors for glutamate, commonly found in its salt form as the food additive monosodium glutamate (MSG).[tpl]cite web|url=http://www.nature.com/neuro/press_release/nn0200.html |title=Umami taste receptor identified |publisher=Nature |accessdate=26 June 2013[/tpl] For that reason, scientists consider umami to be distinct from saltiness.Sweet, Sour, Salty, Bitter ... and Umami, NPR

==Background==

Scientists have debated whether umami was a basic taste since Kikunae Ikeda first proposed its existence in 1908.[ref]
