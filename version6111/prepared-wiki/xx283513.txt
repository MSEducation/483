[[Battle of Constantinople (378)]]

CATEGORIES: 378 in Asia, 4th-century conflicts, Battles involving the Byzantine Empire, Battles involving the Roman Empire, Battles involving the Goths, Constantinople, 4th century in the Byzantine Empire, 370s in the Byzantine Empire

The Battle of Constantinople was a Gothic attack on Constantinople in 378 following the Gothic victory at the Battle of Adrianople. The emperor Valens's widow prepared the defence, and also reinforced the city with Saracens, which performed excellently in combat.[tpl]cite book|author=Jan Retso|title=The Arabs in Antiquity: Their History from the Assyrians to the Umayyads|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=a9RN6gE8z40C&pg=PA518|accessdate=19 August 2013|date=4 July 2013|publisher=Routledge|isbn=978-1-136-87282-2|pages=518–[/tpl][tpl]cite book|author=Alexander Van Millingen|title=Constantinople Painted by Warwick Goble|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=WCfr6NkKKj4C&pg=PT15|accessdate=19 August 2013|publisher=Library of Alexandria|isbn=978-1-4655-2780-6|pages=15–[/tpl][tpl]cite book|author=John Marincola|title=A Companion to Greek and Roman Historiography|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=HXSwRG_whNUC&pg=PT548|accessdate=19 August 2013|date=9 February 2009|publisher=John Wiley & Sons|isbn=978-0-470-76628-6|pages=548–[/tpl] It is said that the Goths were impressed when one of the Saracen warriors stormed out of the city naked, slaugthered enemies and drank blood from the neck of a decapitated Goth. Other sources maintain that the Goths actually abandoned the attack because they were greatly outnumbered.[tpl]cite book|author=Alexander Van Millingen|title=Byzantine Constantinople: The Walls of the City and Adjoining Historical Sites|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=x9N39KZn2QAC&pg=PA40|accessdate=19 August 2013|date=31 October 2010|publisher=Cambridge University Press|isbn=978-1-108-01456-4|pages=40–[/tpl][tpl]cite book|author=Stephen Turnbull|title=The Walls of Constantinople AD 324-1453|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=p4Ahnz2ltv0C&pg=PA5|accessdate=19 August 2013|date=21 August 2012|publisher=Osprey Publishing|isbn=978-1-78200-224-6|pages=5–[/tpl]
In the end, Goths did not enter the city and retreated to Thrace, Illyrium and Dacia.[tpl]cite book|author=Jane Penrose|title=Rome and Her Enemies: An Empire Created and Destroyed by War|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=99haLasvV3gC&pg=PA269|accessdate=19 August 2013|year=2005|publisher=Osprey Publishing|isbn=978-1-84176-932-5|pages=269–[/tpl]
