==Education==

Sue Black was born in Inverness and educated at Inverness Royal Academy.[tpl]citation needed|date=June 2011[/tpl] She then achieved undergraduate and postgraduate degrees at the University of Aberdeen.[tpl]fact|date=March 2014[/tpl]

==Career==

In 1987, she took up the post of lecturer in Anatomy at St. Thomas' Hospital, London which started her career in forensic anthropology and commenced a partnership with Louise Scheuer.[tpl]citation needed|date=June 2010[/tpl]
In 1999, she became the lead forensic anthropologist to the British Forensic Team in Kosovo, deployed by the Foreign and Commonwealth Office on behalf of the United Nations.  
She published Developmental Juvenile Osteology[tpl]cite isbn|0126240000[/tpl] with Louise Scheuer in 2000, and four years later, The Juvenile Skeleton.[tpl]cite isbn|0121028216[/tpl]  These books remain core reading for all students of osteology.[tpl]citation needed|date=June 2011[/tpl] 
Sue Black is head of the Centre for Anatomy and Human Identification at the University of Dundee which runs undergraduate courses in forensic anthropology and postgraduate courses in anatomy and advanced forensic anthropology.[tpl]citation needed|date=June 2011[/tpl] This department is training the UK National Disaster Victim Identification (UK DVI) team for police and scientists in advanced mortuary practices.
Sue is a Director of the Centre for International Forensic Assistance[tpl]cite web | url=http://www.cifa.ac/ |title=Centre for International Forensic Assistance | publisher=CIFA | year=2003 | accessdate=June 16, 2011[/tpl][tpl]cite book|author1=Tim Thompson|author2=Sue Black|title=Forensic Human Identification: An Introduction|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=2o6iyCnwmDEC&pg=PA384|accessdate=18 October 2012|date=14 November 2006|publisher=Taylor & Francis|isbn=978-0-8493-3954-7|pages=384â€“[/tpl]  and a founder of the British Association for Human Identificationhttp://www.bahid.org/home/about-us/ and the British Association for Forensic Anthropology.  She is Deputy Principal for public engagement at the University of Dundee.
She co-authored the 2014 Syrian detainee report.

==Awards and honours==

Black was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society of Edinburgh and a Fellow of the Royal Anthropological Institute. She received the 2008 Lucy Mair Medal from the Royal Anthropological Institute, a police commendation for DVI training and the OBE for her services to forensic anthropology in Kosovo. She has appeared on BBC television.[tpl]cite web | url=http://www.bbc.co.uk/programmes/b00sbjp7 | title=Ipswich Man | publisher=BBC | work=BBC 2 | year=2010 | accessdate=June 16, 2011[/tpl]
In February 2013 she was assessed as one of the 100 most powerful women in the UK by BBC Radio 4's Woman's Hour"Professor Sue Black OBE" at BBC Radio 4, Woman's Hour Power list and in 2014 was also the subject of The Life Scientific on the same station.
