[[Ralph Hills]]

CATEGORIES: American shot putters, Olympic bronze medalists for the United States, Athletes (track and field) at the 1924 Summer Olympics, Olympic track and field athletes of the United States, 1902 births, 1977 deaths, Olympic medalists in athletics (track and field)

Ralph Gorman Hills (January 19, 1902 – September 20, 1977) was an American athlete who competed mainly in the shot put.  After winning a bronze medal for the U.S. at the 1924 Summer Olympics shot put competition, Hills became a physician.

==Early life, education, and career==

Hills, a grandson of U.S. Senator Arthur Pue Gorman, was born to Ralph Warren Hills (1875–1940),[tpl]cite book |url=http://archive.org/details/lextalionisanaly00hill |title=Lex talionis; an analysis of the forces whose resultant produced the Treaty of Versailles |last=Hills |first=Ralph Warren |year=1922 |publisher=Fleet-McGinley |location=Baltimore, Maryland |accessdate=September 7, 2013 [/tpl] a lawyer and diplomat, and Mary Edna Gorman Hills (1876–1944) on January 19, 1902. He attended The Hill School in Pottstown, Pennsylvania, from 1916 through graduation in 1921, where he was on the football team, served as class president, and developed his track athletics skills. Continuing his education at Princeton University, from which he graduated in 1925, Hills was captain of the track team. He pursued his M.D. degree at Johns Hopkins University, married Mary Joe Dixon (daughter of Montana governor Joseph M. Dixon) in 1928, and graduated in 1929. After an internship year at Union Memorial Hospital, and residencies at the Johns Hopkins Hospital, Hills entered private practice. During World War II, Hills served overseas with the U.S. Army's 18th General Hospital, and had the rank of Second Lieutenant when discharged.[tpl]cite web |url=http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2279394/pdf/tacca00099-0040.pdf |title=Memorial: Ralph Gorman Hills |last=Howard |first=John Eager, M.D. |accessdate=August 31, 2013 |format=PDF |publisher=National Institutes of Health United States National Library of Medicine |work=Transactions of the American Clinical and Climatological Association [/tpl]

==Athletics==

Hills first joined an Olympic team at the 1920 Summer Olympics in Antwerp, Belgium.[tpl]cite web |url=http://alumni.thehill.org/about/hall-of-fame/2012-induction-class |title=The Hill School Athletics Hall of Fame Class of 2012 |publisher=The Hill School Alumni Association |year=2012 |accessdate=September 7, 2012 [/tpl] He won a succession of AAU and IC4A collegiate titles, both indoor and outdoor, each year he was at Princeton. In 1924, Hills represented the United States at the 1924 Summer Olympics in Paris, France, and won the bronze medal for the shot put competition.[tpl]cite web |url=http://www.sports-reference.com/olympics/athletes/hi/ralph-hills-1.html |title= Ralph Hills Bio, Stats, and Results |publisher=Sports Reference |accessdate=August 31, 2013 [/tpl]

==Death and legacy==

Hills taught students, staff, and nurses throughout his career, at Johns Hopkins and Union Memorial Hospitals.
He died on September 20, 1977, from complications following a ruptured abdominal aneurysm. Hill's alma mater, The Hill School, inducted him to their Athletics Hall of Fame in 2012.
His first son, J. Dixon Hills, entered the medical profession like his father as a physician.
