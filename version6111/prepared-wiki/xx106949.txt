[[New York Women's House of Detention]]

CATEGORIES: Defunct prisons in New York City, Art Deco architecture in New York City, Women's prisons in the United States

The New York Women's House of Detention was a women's prison in New York City which existed from 1932 to 1974.
Built on the site of the Jefferson Market Prison that had succeeded the Jefferson Market in Manhattan's Greenwich Village,[tpl]cite news|title=Women's house of detention protects the first offenders; New York's model prison|url=http://select.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=F60F1FFD3C54157A93CAA91788D85F458385F9|accessdate=May 18, 2011|newspaper=The New York Times|date=March 8, 1931[/tpl] the New York Women's House of Detention is believed to have been the world's only art deco prison.[tpl]cite journal|last=Gold|first=Ed|title=Where 'The House of D' once loomed, garden blooms|journal=The Villager|year=2005|volume=74|issue=51|url=http://www.thevillager.com/villager_104/wherethehouseofd.html|accessdate=May 18, 2011[/tpl]  It was designed by Sloan & Robertson in 1931 at a cost of $2,000,000 and opened to the public by Richard C. Patterson, Jr. on March 29, 1932.[tpl]cite news|title=Luxury jail here for women ready|url=http://select.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=FA0714FC345A16738DDDA00A94DB405B828FF1D3|accessdate=May 18, 2011|newspaper=The New York Times|date=March 29, 1932[/tpl]  It did not receive its first inmates until some time later.  Its location at 10 Greenwich Avenue gave the women inmates an opportunity to try to communicate with people walking by.[tpl]cite news|last=Kaufman|first=Michael T.|title=Davis case goes to city's courts|url=http://select.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=F20D17F7345C1B7493C7A8178BD95F448785F9|accessdate=May 18, 2011|newspaper=The New York Times|date=October 15, 1970[/tpl] After the prison was officially closed on June 13, 1971,[tpl]cite news|last=Spiegel|first=Irving|title=Women's prison closed; Inmates moved to Rikers|url=http://select.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=F6091FF73A5B137A93C6A8178DD85F458785F9|accessdate=May 18, 2011|newspaper=The New York Times|date=June 14, 1971[/tpl] Mayor Lindsay began the demolition of the prison in 1973,[tpl]cite news|last=Schumach|first=Murray|title=City begins demolishing women's jail|url=http://select.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=F60F1EF83D5D127A93C2A8178BD95F478785F9|accessdate=May 18, 2011|newspaper=The New York Times|date=October 10, 1973[/tpl] and it was completed the following year.[tpl]cite news|last=Neumaier|first=Joe|title=Duchovny: Film is both personal and universal|url=http://seattletimes.nwsource.com/html/movies/2002250324_duchovny23.html|accessdate=May 18, 2011|newspaper=The Seattle Times|date=April 23, 2005[/tpl] It was replaced with a garden.[tpl]cite news|last=Schaefer|first=Stephen|title=The Duchovny files; 'X-Files' star steps out on a limb with directorial debut|url=http://pqasb.pqarchiver.com/bostonherald/access/827275991.html?dids=827275991:827275991&FMT=ABS&FMTS=ABS:FT&type=current&date=Apr+26%2C+2005&author=STEPHEN+SCHAEFER&pub=Boston+Herald&desc=MOVIES%3B+The+Duchovny+files%3B+%60X-Files'+star+steps+out+on+a+limb+with+directorial+debut&pqatl=google|accessdate=May 18, 2011|newspaper=Boston Herald|date=April 26, 2005[/tpl]
Ruth E. Collins was the first superintendent at the prison.[tpl]cite news|last=Blanshard|first=Julia|title=Modern skyscraper prison will be "school" for women|url=http://news.google.com/newspapers?id=RrpIAAAAIBAJ&sjid=jwENAAAAIBAJ&pg=913,792505&dq=new+york+women's+house+of+detention+ruth+e+collins&hl=en|accessdate=May 18, 2011|newspaper=The Meridan Daily Journal|date=January 9, 1932[/tpl]  She embraced the design of the prison, labeling it "a new era in penology".  Her mission was to effect the moral and social rehabilitation of the women in her charge, giving them a chance for "restoration as well as for punishment".
