[[Hanlon's razor]]

CATEGORIES: Adages, Critical thinking, Principles, Razors (philosophy)

Hanlon's razor is an eponymous adage that allows the elimination of unlikely explanations for a phenomenon. It reads:

This particular form is attributed to a Robert J. Hanlon. However, earlier utterances that convey the same basic idea are known.

Origins and etymology

The adage was popularized in this form and under this name by the Jargon File, a glossary of computer programmer slang.
The name was inspired by Occam's razor. 1 Later that same year, the Jargon File editors noted lack of knowledge about the term's derivation, and the existence of a similar epigram by William James.
In 2001, Quentin Stafford-Fraser published two blog entries citing e-mails from one Joseph E. Bigler

"Heinlein's Razor" has since been defined as variations on Never attribute to malice that which can be adequately explained by stupidity, but don't rule out malice. This quotation is attributed to Albert Einstein in Peter W. Singer's 2009 book Wired for War.
A similar saying, patterned after Arthur C. Clarke's famous comment about technology and magic, has often been called Grey's Law: "Any sufficiently advanced incompetence is indistinguishable from malice".  The origin of this attribution is unknown.
