Life and career

Powers was born in Morristown, Vermont to Dr. Horace Powers and Love E. Gilman Powers. Powers studied law, was admitted to the bar in 1858, and practiced first in Hyde Park, and later in Morristown.
He served as a member of the Vermont House of Representatives in 1858. Powers was the Lamoille County State's Attorney in 1861 and 1862.
Powers served in the Vermont State Senate in 1872 and 1873.
Powers served as Judge of the Vermont Supreme Court from December 1874 to December 1890.
Powers served as a member of the Lamoille County Bank board of directors from 1888 until his death. He was a Delegate to the 1892 Republican National Convention.
Powers was elected as a Republican to the Fifty-second United States Congress and to the four succeeding Congresses, serving from March 4, 1891 until March 3, 1901. In 1896 he sponsored a controversial funding bill that would have allowed the Central Pacific Railroad to avoid paying of a thirty-year-old debt to the government. The bill inspired a campaign of opposition led by publisher William Randolph Hearst and his employee, Ambrose Bierce.
Powers was an unsuccessful candidate for renomination in 1900. After leaving Congress, he resumed the practice of law in Morrisville, Vermont, and was the chief counsel for the Rutland Railroad.

Personal life

Powers married Caroline Waterman on October 11, 1858.

Death and legacy

He died in Morristown on December 8, 1913, and is interred in Pleasant View Cemetery. 
Powers home in Morrisville, Vermont is on the National Register of Historic Places by the United States Department of the Interior. The home was built around 1850 and is named the Horace Henry Powers House.
