Infrastructure

Industrialisation occurred early in Central Europe. That caused construction of rail and other types of infrastructure.

=Rail=

Rail infrastructure is the densest in the world. Railway density, with total length of lines operated (km) per 1,000 km2, is the highest in the Czech Republic (198.6), Poland (121.0), Slovenia (108.0), Germany (105.5), Hungary (98.7), Romania (85.9), Slovakia (73.9), Croatia (72.5) and Serbia (49.2)

Branches

Compared to most of Europe, the economies of Germany, Switzerland, Austria, Czech Republic, Slovenia, Hungary, the Slovak Republic, Poland, Croatia and Romania tend to demonstrate high complexity. Industrialisation has reached Central Europe relatively early: Luxembourg and Germany by 1860, the Czech Republic, Poland, Slovakia and Switzerland by 1870, Austria, Croatia Hungary, Liechtenstein, Romania, Serbia, and Slovenia by 1880.sru.hughes/100/100-5/d-5-19.htm

Culture

Cuisine

Central European cuisine has evolved through centuries due to social and political change. Most countries share many dishes. The most popular dishes typical to Central Europe are sausages and cheeses, where the earliest evidence of cheesemaking in the archaeological record dates back to 5,500 BCE (Kujawy, Poland).nature.12020 Other foods widely associated with Central Europe are goulash and beer. List of countries by beer consumption per capita is led by the Czech Republic, followed by Germany and Austria. Poland comes 9th, Slovenia 12th, Croatia 15th, and Romania 16th.

Beliefs

Central Europe has been a centre of Protestantism in the past, however it has been mostly eradicated by the Counterreformation.hrw.nd/gohrw_rls1/pKeywordResults?ST9%20Eur%20Religions%201600 Central European countries are mostly Catholic (Austria, Croatia, Liechtenstein, Luxembourg, Slovakia, Slovenia), historically Catholic (the Czech Republic), Protestant or mixed Catholic and Protestant (Germany, Hungary and Switzerland). Serbia and Romania are mostly Orthodox with significant Protestant and Catholic minorities.
