[[Žinčica]]

CATEGORIES: Dairy products, Fermented beverages, Polish cuisine, Slovak cuisine

Žinčica (in Slovak) or Žinčice (in Czech), Żentyca (in Polish)[tpl]cite web |url=http://www.minrol.gov.pl/DesktopModules/Announcement/ViewAnnouncement.aspx?ModuleID=1276&TabOrgID=1530&LangId=0&AnnouncementId=3987&ModulePositionId=1747 |title=Żentyca |date=6 October 2005 |publisher=Ministry of Agriculture and Rural Development of the Republic of Poland |language=Polish |accessdate=2 March 2009[/tpl] [tpl]Dead link|date=October 2010|bot=H3llBot[/tpl] is a drink made of sheep milk whey similar to kefir.[tpl]cite encyclopedia |title=Žinčica |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=ucPf5kCNGjMC&pg=PA322 |first1=Joseph A. |last1=Kurmann |author2=Jeremija Lj Rašić |first3=Manfred |last3=Kroger |encyclopedia=Encyclopedia of Fermented Fresh Milk Products: An International Inventory of Fermented Milk, Cream, Buttermilk, Whey, and Related Products |publisher=Van Nostrand Reinhold |location=New York City |year=1992 |pages=322–323 |isbn=0-442-00869-4 |oclc=24469241 |accessdate=2009-03-02[/tpl] It is a by-product in the process of making bryndza cheese.
Žinčica is fermented by the following Lactic acid bacteria: Lactobacillus casei, Lactobacillus plantarum, Lactococcus lactis and Leuconostoc mesenteroides.
Traditionally, this drink is served in a črpák, a wooden cup with some pastoral scene carved into the handle.[tpl]cite web |url=http://www.slovakia.travel/entitaview.aspx?l=2&idp=16680 |title=Mountain sheep farming (Salašníctvo) |first=Tomáš |last=Belko |publisher=Slovenskej agentúry pre cestovný ruch |accessdate=2 March 2009[/tpl][tpl]cite web |url=http://www.artificium.sk/page.php?al=crpak |title=Črpáky |date=6 October 2006 |accessdate=2 March 2009 |language=Slovak[/tpl]
Bryndzové halušky is typically served with žinčica.[tpl]cite book |first1=Neal |last1=Bedford |first2=Jane |last2=Rawson |first3=Matt |last3=Warren |title=Lonely Planet Czech & Slovak Republics |publisher=Lonely Planet |location=Hawthorn, Victoria |year=2004 |page=62 |isbn=1-74104-046-9 |oclc=55129941[/tpl]
The origin of the word is the Romanian jîntiţa, the drink being carried by Vlach shepherds instead of water.[tpl]Citation needed|date=March 2009[/tpl]
