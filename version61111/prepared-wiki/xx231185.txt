In terms of style and age, it is comparable to the Empress Theodora's Codex.

==History==

The origin of this manuscript has been and remains the subject of debates. It was found in a church of Berat, and became known after publication written by bishop of Berat “Description abrégée et historique de la sainte métropole de Belgrade, aujourd’hui Berat” (Corfu, 1868). It was examined by Pierre Batiffol.Pierre Batiffol, Les manuscrits grecs de Berat d'Albanie et le Codex Purpureus, Paris 1886.
Formerly the codex was located in a church in Berat, since 1971 it is housed in the National Archives of Albania (No. 2) at Tirana.[tpl]r|Aland[/tpl][tpl]Cite web|url=http://intf.uni-muenster.de/vmr/NTVMR/ListeHandschriften.php|title=Liste Handschriften|publisher=Institute for New Testament Textual Research|accessdate=16 November 2010|location=Münster[/tpl] Codex Beratinus 2 now is registered with the UNESCO as a world treasure.
