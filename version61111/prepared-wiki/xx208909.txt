[[Barry Keenan]]

CATEGORIES: 1940 births, Living people, American businesspeople, American kidnappers, American businesspeople convicted of crimes

Barry Keenan (born 1940) is an American businessman who is best known as the mastermind behind the 1963 kidnapping of Frank Sinatra, Jr.
At the age of 21, Keenan was already successful in the world of business, as well as being the youngest member of the Los Angeles Stock Exchange.[tpl]cite web|url=http://www.crimelibrary.com/notorious_murders/classics/root/12.html|title=The Sinatra Kidnapping Case|last=Courtroom Television Network, LLC|publisher=Turner Entertainment|accessdate=2008-07-26| archiveurl= http://web.archive.org/web/20080709072812/http://www.crimelibrary.com/notorious_murders/classics/root/12.html| archivedate= 9 July 2008 | deadurl= no[/tpl] A car accident left Keenan penniless and addicted to painkillers, eventually leading him to hatch the scheme and enlist help in the kidnap-for-ransom plan.[tpl]cite web|url=http://www.roanoke.com/news/roanoke/wb/65576|title=Man who stole Sinatra|last=Allen|first=Mike|date=2006-05-18|publisher=The Roanoke Times|accessdate=2008-07-26[/tpl] Keenan, along with Johnny Irwin and Joe Amsler, conspired to kidnap Frank Sinatra, Jr.[tpl]cite news|url=http://query.nytimes.com/gst/fullpage.html?res=9C0CE4DF143AF930A15757C0A9629C8B63|title=TELEVISION REVIEW; Unkindness Of Strangers (Doobie-Doobie-Doo) |last=Heffernan|first=Virginia|date=2004-08-23|publisher=The New York Times Company  |accessdate=2008-07-26[/tpl] Keenan had a psychiatric condition in which he heard voices, and felt that his plan was blessed by God; since he intended to eventually pay the money back, he did not think the kidnapping was immoral.This American Life Episode 205: Plan B, "Act Three. Kidnapping as Plan B." After successfully nabbing Sinatra Jr. from the Harrah's Lodge at South Lake Tahoe, Keenan made contact with Frank Sinatra, Sr. to make ransom arrangements. Sinatra initially offered one million dollars, but Keenan demanded $240,000 instead; significantly less.[tpl]cite news|url=http://www.sierrasun.com/article/20051208/LIFE/112080009|title=Kidnapped: The search for Frank Sinatra Jr.|last=McLaughlin|first=Mark |date=2005-12-08|accessdate=2008-07-27[/tpl] Despite the nature of the crime, Keenan felt that he was bringing the Sinatra family closer together and assisting the Sinatras in other intangible ways.
Sinatra paid the ransom and the three men released his son. Within days, all three conspirators were apprehended by the FBI. Keenan was sentenced to life plus seventy-five years in prison for his crimes, but only served four and a half years before he was released,[tpl]cite news|url=http://findarticles.com/p/articles/mi_qa5277/is_199801/ai_n24340114|title=Deer Island development proposal brings up old Sinatra kidnapping scandal|last=The Mississippi Business Journal|accessdate=2008-07-27 | work=The Mississippi Business Journal | year=1998[/tpl] because it was determined that he was legally insane at the time of the crime.
After his release, Keenan went on to become successful as a real estate developer.[tpl]cite web|url=http://www.history.com/this-day-in-history.do?action=Article&id=1214|title=Frank Sinatra Jr. endures a frightening ordeal|last=A&E Television Networks|accessdate=2008-07-27[/tpl] He is currently writing a book about the affair, with all proceeds going to charity, due for publication in 2013. The event was made into the basis for a Showtime movie released in 2003, Stealing Sinatra, starring David Arquette and William H. Macy
