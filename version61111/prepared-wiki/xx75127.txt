</ref> Jervis spent the first few years of the war patrolling the Channel in ''Foudroyant'' without seeing any significant action, but as the war reached Europe Jervis was placed under the command of Admiral [[Augustus Keppel, 1st Viscount Keppel|Augustus Keppel]]. The Channel fleet, under Keppel, sighted the French fleet intending to enter [[Brest, France|Brest]] on 23 July. The British fleet of 30 ships of the line chased the French fleet of 29 ships and engaged them on 27 July in what became known as the [[Battle of Ushant (1778)|First Battle of Ushant]].<ref name="Tuck48">Tucker. Vol. 1, p.48</ref><ref name="NC06/7">''The Naval Chronicle'' Vol. 4, pp.6–7</ref> The battle was indecisive and in the political aftermath Jervis provided a stalwart defence of Admiral Keppel at the latter's [[court-martial]], helping to secure Keppel's acquittal.<ref name="Tuck49">Tucker. Vol. 1, pp.49–67</ref><ref name="NC07">''The Naval Chronicle'' Vol. 4, p.7</ref>

===Relieving Gibraltar and capture of the Pégase===

Jervis remained in Foudroyant attached to the Channel Fleet and for a short time acted as flag captain to Admiral Molyneux Shuldham.Tucker. Vol. 1, p.68 In 1780 Jervis was with Admiral Rodney when the British fleet relieved Gibraltar. In 1781 he was with Admiral George Darby at the second relief of Gibraltar. On 19 April 1782 Jervis was with his old friend and travelling companion when a ship in Admiral Barrington’s squadron sighted a French convoy leaving Brest. The squadron gave chase and Foudroyant caught and engaged the French 74-gun Pégase.Brenton. Vol.1, p.63[tpl]cite book |last=Winfield |title=British Warships in the Age of Sail 1714–1792: Design, Construction, Careers and Fates|page=68[/tpl] After an engagement of more than an hour Pégase struck.The Naval Chronicle Vol. 4, pp.9–10 Jervis himself was wounded in the attack.Tucker. Vol. 1, p.76 For his services he was invested as a Knight of the Bath on 19 May 1782.[tpl]London Gazette|issue=13694 |date=1782-28-05 |startpage=4|endpage=|accessdate=14 July 2010[/tpl] He was again at the relief of Gibraltar with Earl Howe's fleet in 1782The Naval Chronicle Vol. 4, p.11 and took part in the indecisive Battle of Cape Spartel.Tucker. Vol. 1, p.77 Jervis was promoted commodore and hoisted his broad pennant in the 50-gun [tpl]HMS|Salisbury|1769|6[/tpl] in December 1782, with orders to proceed to the West Indies. Due to the peace negotiations his orders were rescinded and he struck his pennant on 14 January 1783.

==Marriage and political office==

During the peace Jervis married his cousin Martha, daughter of Lord Chief Baron Sir Thomas Parker.Tucker. Vol. 1, p.91Brenton. Vol.1, p.14 Jervis was also returned as MP for Launceston in 1783.Tucker. Vol. 1, p.92 Jervis began his political career in earnest and voted for Pitt's parliamentary reforms and against Charles James Fox and his East India Bill. During the elections of 1784 Jervis stood for election in the independent borough of Great Yarmouth where he was returned as MP alongside Henry Beaufoy.[tpl]London Gazette|issue=12537 |date=1784-24-04 |startpage=1|endpage=|accessdate=14 July 2010[/tpl] Jervis then voted against Pitt's further bills for reform but supported him once more during the 1788–1799 Regency Crisis.
On 24 September 1787 Jervis was promoted rear-admiral of the blue[tpl]London Gazette|issue=12924 |date=1797-25-09 |startpage=446|endpage=|accessdate=13 July 2010[/tpl]Tucker. Vol. 1, p.96The Naval Chronicle Vol. 4, p.12 and hoisted his flag in the 74-gun Carnatic for several months during the tensions arising from the Prussian invasion of the Netherlands.Tucker. Vol. 1, p.96–97 In 1790 Jervis was recalled to service once more and moved his flag to the second-rate Prince during the Nootka Sound crisis that threatened war between England and Spain.Tucker. Vol. 1, p.97–99 Also in 1790 Jervis was promoted rear-admiral of the white[tpl]London Gazette|issue=13238 |date=1790-18-09 |startpage=582|endpage=|accessdate=14 July 2010[/tpl] and stood down as MP for Great Yarmouth and stood instead for the Chipping Wycombe seat to which he was returned as MP alongside the Marquess of Lansdowne.Tucker. Vol. 1, p.101[tpl]London Gazette|issue=13226 |date=8 July 1790 |startpage=503|endpage=|accessdate=14 July 2010[/tpl] With his interest in politics wavering he spoke rarely and then almost exclusively on naval matters. In 1794 he resigned his seat and did not stand again for political office.Tucker. Vol. 1, p.102 In 1792 Jervis proposed a scheme to alleviate the financial hardship of superannuated seamen.Brenton. Vol.1, p.86 He later withdrew the proposal as Viscount Melville promised that the matter would be addressed by the Admiralty Board.Brenton. Vol.1, p.89
