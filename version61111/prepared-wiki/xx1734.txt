</ref> [[Economist]] [[Jeffrey Sachs]] described the [[United States]] as a corporatocracy in his book ''[[The Price of Civilization (book)|The Price of Civilization]]''.<ref name="sachs price of civilization">[tpl]cite book  | last = Sachs  | first = Jeffrey  | title = ''The Price of Civilization''  | publisher = Random House  | year = 2011  | location = New York  | pages = 105, 106, 107  | isbn = 978-1-4000-6841-8[/tpl]</ref> He suggested that it arose from four trends: weak [[Political parties in the United States|national parties]] and strong political representation of individual districts, the large U.S. military establishment after [[World War II]], big corporate money financing election campaigns, and [[globalization]] tilting the balance away from workers.<ref name="sachs price of civilization"/>
This collective is known as what author C Wright Mills would call the Power Elite. The Power Elite are wealthy individuals who hold prominent positions in Corporatocracies. These individuals control the process of determining society's economic and political policies.[tpl]cite book| last = Doob| first = Christopher| title = Social Inequality and Social Stratification | publisher = Pearson | edition = 1st ed.| year = 2013| location = Boston| pages = 143| isbn =  [/tpl]
The concept has been used in explanations of bank bailouts, excessive pay for CEOs, as well as complaints such as the exploitation of national treasuries, people, and natural resources. ==Historical corporatocracies==

Corporations have held the right to vote in some jurisdictions.  For example, Livery Companies currently appoint most of the voters for the City of London Corporation, which is the municipal government for the area centered on the financial district.
