[tpl]nihongo|Hiragana|Âπ≥‰ªÆÂêç, „Å≤„Çâ„Åå„Å™|[/tpl] is a Japanese syllabary, one basic component of the Japanese writing system, along with katakana, kanji, and in some cases r≈çmaji (the Latin-script alphabet). 
Hiragana and katakana are both kana systems; they have corresponding character sets in which each kana, or character, represents one mora (one sound in the Japanese language). Each kana is either a vowel such as "a" (hiragana „ÅÇ); a consonant followed by a vowel such as "ka" (hiragana „Åã); or "n" (hiragana „Çì), a nasal sonorant which, depending on the context, sounds either like English m, n, or ng ([tpl]IPAblink|≈ã[/tpl]), or like the nasal vowels of French. Because the characters of the kana do not represent single consonants (except in the case of „Çì "n"), the kana are referred to as syllabaries and not alphabets.[tpl]cite book|title=An Introduction to Modern Japanese: Book 1|year=2004|publisher=Cambridge University Press|location=United Kingdom|isbn=978-0521548878|author=Richard Bowring|coauthors=Haruko Uryu Laurie|page=9[/tpl]  
Hiragana is used to write native words for which there are no kanji, including grammatical particles such as [tpl]nowrap|„Åã„Çâ[/tpl] kara "from", and suffixes such as [tpl]nowrap|„Åï„Çì[/tpl] ~san "Mr., Mrs., Miss, Ms." Likewise, hiragana is used to write words whose kanji form is obscure, not known to the writer or readers, or too formal for the writing purpose. There is also some flexibility for words that have common kanji renditions to be optionally written instead in hiragana, according to an individual author's preference. Verb and adjective inflections, as, for example, be-ma-shi-ta [tpl]nowrap|(„Åπ„Åæ„Åó„Åü)[/tpl] in [tpl]nihongo|tabemashita|È£ü„Åπ„Åæ„Åó„Åü||"ate"[/tpl], are written in hiragana, often following a verb or adjective root (here, "È£ü") that is written in kanji. When Hiragana is used to show the pronunciation of kanji characters as reading aid, it is referred to as furigana. The article Japanese writing system discusses in detail how the various systems of writing are used.
There are two main systems of ordering hiragana, the old-fashioned iroha ordering, and the more prevalent goj≈´on ordering.

The forms of the hiragana originate from the cursive script style of Chinese calligraphy. The figure below shows the derivation of hiragana from many≈çgana via cursive script. 320px|right When they were first developed, hiragana were not accepted by everyone. The educated or elites preferred to use only the kanji system. Historically, in Japan, the regular script (kaisho) form of the characters was used by men and called [tpl]nihongo|otokode|Áî∑Êâã|[/tpl], "men's writing", while the cursive script (s≈çsho) form of the kanji was used by women. Hence hiragana first gained popularity among women, who were generally not allowed access to the same levels of education as men. And thus hiragana was first widely used among court women in the writing of personal communications and literature.[tpl]cite book|title=An Introduction to Modern Japanese: Book 1|year=2004|publisher=Cambridge University Press|location=United Kingdom|isbn=978-0521548878|author=Richard Bowring|coauthors=Haruko Uryu Laurie|page=8[/tpl]  From this comes the alternative name of [tpl]nihongo|onnade|Â•≥Êâã|[/tpl] "women's writing".[tpl]cite book|last=Hatasa|first=Yukiko Abe|title=Nakama 1: Introductory Japanese: Communication, Culture, Context 2nd ed.|year=2010|publisher=Heinle|isbn=0495798185|page=2|coauthors=Kazumi Hatasa, Seiichi Makino[/tpl]  For example, The Tale of Genji and other early novels by female authors used hiragana extensively or exclusively.
Male authors came to write literature using hiragana. Hiragana was used for unofficial writing such as personal letters, while katakana and Chinese were used for official documents. In modern times, the usage of hiragana has become mixed with katakana writing. Katakana is now relegated to special uses such as recently borrowed words (i.e., since the 19th century), names in transliteration, the names of animals, in telegrams, and for emphasis.
Originally, for all syllables there was more than one possible hiragana. In 1900, the system was simplified so each syllable had only one hiragana. The deprecated hiragana are now known as [tpl]nihongo|hentaigana|Â§â‰Ωì‰ªÆÂêç|[/tpl].
The pangram poem Iroha-uta ("ABC song/poem"), which dates to the 10th century, uses every hiragana once (except n „Çì, which was just a variant of „ÇÄ before Muromachi era).
