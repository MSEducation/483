Colonel Sir Claude Maxwell MacDonald GCMG GCVO KCB PC (1852 – September 1915) was a British diplomat, best known for his service in China and Japan.Nish, Ian. (2004). British Envoys in Japan 1859-1972, pp. 94-102.

==Early life==

MacDonald was born to a high-ranking officer in the British Army,Kowner,  Historical Dictionary of the Russo-Japanese War, p. 214. and was educated at Uppingham School and Sandhurst. He was commissioned into the 74th Foot in 1872. He thought of himself as a 'soldier-outsider', as regards the Foreign Office.

==Africa==

MacDonald’s early career was in Africa.  He served in the 1882 Anglo-Egyptian War, and served as military attaché to Sir Evelyn Baring from 1884-1885. From 1887 to 1889 he was consul-general at Zanzibar, and then served some years as consul-general at Brass in the West African Oil Rivers Protectorate,Dictionary of National Biography where in 1895 he was an observer of the rebellion of King Koko of Nembe.Sir W. Geary, Nigeria under British Rule (1927), pp. 194-196 He retired from the British Army in 1896.

==China and Korea==

In 1896, MacDonald was appointed British minister to Qing Dynasty Empire of China.  He was also simultaneously the British Minister to the Empire of Korea in 1896 through 1898.Korean Mission to the Conference on the Limitation of Armament, Washington, D.C., 1921-1922. (1922). [tpl]Google books|9OdAAAAAYAAJ|Korea's Appeal, p. 32.|page=32[/tpl]
In China, MacDonald obtained a lease at Weihaiwei, and obtained railway contracts for British syndicates.  He was instrumental in securing the Second Peking Convention, by which China leased to Britain the New Territories of Hong Kong.

===The Macartney-Macdonald Line===

In 1899 MacDonald was the author of a Diplomatic Note which proposed a new demarcation of the border between China and British India in the Karakoram and Kashmir, now known as the Macartney-MacDonald Line, which still forms the basis of the border between China and Pakistan.Mohan Guruswamy, Mohan, "The Great India-China Game," Rediff. June 23, 2003.
As a military man, MacDonald led the defence of the foreign legations in 1900 which were under siege during the Boxer Rebellion, and he worked well with the Anglophile Japanese Colonel Shiba Goro.

==Japan==

MacDonald was appointed Consul-General for the Empire of Japan in October 1900.[tpl]LondonGazette |issue=27263 |date=4 January 1901 |startpage=81 |accessdate=03-11-2012 [/tpl] He presided over the Tokyo Legation in years of harmony between Britain and Japan (1900 to 1912), swapping posts with Sir Ernest Satow who replaced him as Minister in Peking. On 30 January 1902, the first Anglo-Japanese Alliance was signed in London between the Foreign Secretary Lord Lansdowne and Hayashi Tadasu, the Japanese Minister. MacDonald was still in Tokyo when the alliance was renewed in 1905 and 1911. He also became Britain's first ambassador to Japan when the status of the legation was raised to an embassy in 1905.The first British Ambassador to Japan was appointed in 1905. Before 1905, the senior British diplomat had different titles: (a) Consul-General and Envoy Extraordinary and Minister Plenipotentiary, which is a rank just below Ambassador.
MacDonald was made a Privy Councillor in 1906. He died in London in 1915.
