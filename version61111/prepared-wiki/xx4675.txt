As of 2013, there are 346 civilian airports in India -  253 with paved runways and 93 with unpaved runways. Of these, 66 are licensed by the DGCA in the public use category while 21 are licensed for private use.http://dgca.nic.in/aerodrome/aero_list.pdf Most of these are managed by the AAI while the rest are owned by State Governments and private entities. There are more than 20 international airports in India. Indira Gandhi International Airport, Delhi and Chhatrapati Shivaji International Airport, Mumbai handle more than a half of the air traffic in the country.[tpl]cite news|url=http://articles.timesofindia.indiatimes.com/2008-07-10/india/27923943_1_flights-airport-load-factors|work=Online edition of the Times of India, dated 2008-07-10|title=Delhi beats Mumbai to become busiest airport|author=Saurabh Sinha|accessdate=5 June 2009|date=10 July 2008[/tpl][tpl]cite web|url=http://www.domain-b.com/aero/airports/20080901_csia.html |title=Delhi's IGIA edges ahead of Mumbai's CSIA as country's busiest airport |publisher=Domain-b.com |date=1 September 2008 |accessdate=5 April 2010[/tpl][ref][tpl]cite web|url=http://www.travelbizmonitor.com/ArticleDetails.aspx?aid=1777&sid=18&sname=Coverstory |title=Mumbai airport gets ready for new innings |publisher=Travel Biz Monitor |date=24 December 2007 |accessdate=5 April 2010[/tpl]
[/ref]
The operations of the major airports in India have been privatised over the past 5 years and this has resulted in better equipped and cleaner airports. The terminals have either been refurbished or expanded.

==Ports and shipping==

There are many major ports in India. Maritime transportation in India is managed by the Shipping Corporation of India, a government-owned company that also manages offshore and other marine transport infrastructure in the country. It owns and operates about 35% of Indian tonnage and operates in practically all areas of shipping business servicing both national and international trades.[tpl]cite web|url=http://www.shipindia.com/newsite/DisplayContent.asp?CategoryID=1&ContentID=1|title=About Us|work=Official webpage of the Shipping Corporation of India|accessdate=3 June 2009 |archiveurl = http://web.archive.org/web/20071106162636/http://www.shipindia.com/newsite/DisplayContent.asp?CategoryID=1&ContentID=1 |archivedate = 6 November 2007[/tpl]
It has a fleet of 79 ships of 2750,000 GT (4.8 million DWT) and also manages 53 research, survey and support vessels of 120,000 GT (060,000 DWT) on behalf of various government departments and other organisations. Personnel are trained at the Maritime Training Institute in Mumbai, a branch of the World Maritime University, which was set up in 1987.[tpl]cite web|url=http://www.shipindia.com/newsite/default.asp?CategoryID=7|title=The Maritime Training Institute (MTI)|work=Official webpage of the Shipping Corporation of India|accessdate=3 June 2009 |archiveurl = http://web.archive.org/web/20080607135935/http://www.shipindia.com/newsite/default.asp?CategoryID=7 |archivedate = 7 June 2008[/tpl] The Corporation also operates in Malta and Iran through joint ventures.
The ports are the main centres of trade. In India about 95% of the foreign trade by quantity and 70% by value takes place through the ports.[tpl]cite web | url = http://www.ibef.org/download/Infra_Brochure_Spread.pdf | format = PDF | title = Discover Opportunity: Infrastructure in India | publisher = India Brand Equity Foundation (IBEF), An initiative of the Ministry of Commerce & Industry, Government of India | page = 6 | accessdate = 1 June 2009[/tpl] Mumbai Port & JNPT(Navi Mumbai) handles 70% of maritime trade in India.[tpl]cite web|url=http://business.rediff.com/slide-show/2010/aug/11/slide-show-1-worst-oil-spills-in-the-world.htm |title=10 worst oil spills that cost trillions in losses : Rediff.com Business |publisher=Business.rediff.com |accessdate=16 August 2010[/tpl] There are twelve major ports: Navi Mumbai, Mumbai, Kochi, Kolkata (including Haldia), Paradip, Visakhapatnam, Ennore, Chennai, Tuticorin, New Mangalore, Mormugao and Kandla.[tpl]cite book | title = Manorama Yearbook 2009 | editor1-last = Mathew | editor1-first = K. M. | publisher = Malayala Manorama | page = 606 | chapter = India: Transportation | isbn = 81-89004-12-3 | accessdate = 1 June 2009[/tpl] Other than these, there are 187 minor and intermediate ports, 43 of which handle cargo.
The distinction between major and minor ports is not based on the amount of cargo handled. The major ports are managed by port trusts which are regulated by the central government.[tpl]citation needed|date=July 2012[/tpl] They come under the purview of the Major Port Trusts Act, 1963.[tpl]citation needed|date=July 2012[/tpl] The minor ports are regulated by the respective state governments and many of these ports are private ports or captive ports.[tpl]citation needed|date=July 2012[/tpl] The total amount of traffic handled at the major ports in 2005-2006 was 382.33 Mt.

==Environmental issues and impact==

The National capital New Delhi has one of the largest CNG based transport systems as a part of the drive to bring down pollution. In spite of these efforts it remains the largest contributor to the greenhouse gas emissions in the city.[tpl]cite web|url=http://www.indianexpress.com/news/delhis-green-transport-is-its-main-polluter-finds-report/555143/0|title=Delhi’s green transport is its main polluter, finds report|accessdate =23 December 2009|location=Bangalore[/tpl] The CNG Bus manufacturers in India are Ashok Leyland, Tata Motors, Swaraj Mazda and Hindustan Motors.[tpl]cite web|url=http://www.cleanairnet.org/caiasia/1412/article-58782.html|title=CNG Bus Manufacturers in India|accessdate =23 December 2009|location=Bangalore, India[/tpl]
In 1998, the Supreme Court of India published a Directive that specified the date of April 2001 as deadline to replace or convert all buses, three-wheelers and taxis in Delhi to compressed natural gas.[tpl]cite web|url=http://www.product-life.org/en/archive/cng-delhi|title=CNG Delhi– the world’s cleanest public bus system running on CNG|first=Anumita Roychaudhary|year=1998|publisher=Product-Life Institute|language=Eng|accessdate=23 November 2009|location=Geneva[/tpl]
The Karnataka State Road Transport Corporation was the first State Transport Undertaking in India to utilise bio-fuels and ethanol-blended fuels.[tpl]cite web|url=http://www.ksrtc.in/BestPractices.html|title=KSRTC :: BEST PRACTICES|publisher=Karnataka State Road Transport Corporation|accessdate=23 November 2009|location=Karnataka, India|archiveurl=http://web.archive.org/web/20091117194311/http://ksrtc.in/BestPractices.html |archivedate=17 November 2009[/tpl] KSRTC took an initiative to do research in alternative fuel forms by experimenting with various alternatives— blending diesel with biofuels such as honge, palm, sunflower, groundnut, coconut and sesame.[tpl]cite news|url=http://www.hindu.com/2007/01/31/stories/2007013117930300.htm|title=KSRTC going that extra mile to conserve fuel|first=Anil Kumar Sastry|date=31 January 2007|publisher=Online edition of The Hindu|language=Eng|accessdate=23 November 2009|location=Karnataka India[/tpl] In 2009, the corporation decided to promote the use of biofuel buses.[tpl]cite news|url=http://www.hindu.com/2009/06/06/stories/2009060661240400.htm|title=State to promote biofuel buses|date=6 Jun 2009|publisher=The Hindu|language=Eng|accessdate=23 November 2009|location=KAR, IND[/tpl]
