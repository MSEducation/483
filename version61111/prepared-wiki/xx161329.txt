[[H. Henry Powers]]

Horace Henry Powers (May 29, 1835 – December 8, 1913) was an American lawyer, judge and politician. He served as a U.S. Representative from Vermont.

==Life and career==

Powers was born in Morristown, Vermont to Dr. Horace Powers and Love E. Gilman Powers.[tpl]cite web|url= http://www.history50states.com/VT-Lamoille-Morristown|title =Horace Henry Powers Biography|publisher= History50States.com |accessdate= December 21, 2012[/tpl] He graduated from People's Academy, and received a bachelor's degree from the University of Vermont in 1855. He earned his master's degree from UVM in 1858.[tpl]cite book|last=Johnson|first=Rossiter and John Howard Brown|title=The Twentieth Century Biographical Dictionary of Notable Americans|year=1904|publisher=Biographical Society|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=ElIDAAAAYAAJ&pg=PT408&lpg=PT408&dq=h.+henry+powers+Morristown,+Vermont&source=bl&ots=ok46o83orp&sig=vtKPkBfGmj-N22S8nlUiOmA5LTw&hl=en&sa=X&ei=HgvWUK6QLKPi2gWm0YDIBQ&ved=0CHIQ6AEwCQ[/tpl] Powers studied law, was admitted to the bar in 1858, and practiced first in Hyde Park, and later in Morristown.
He served as a member of the Vermont House of Representatives in 1858. Powers was the Lamoille County State's Attorney in 1861 and 1862.[tpl]cite book|last=Secretary of State|title=Vermont Legislative Directory|year=1884|publisher=Secretary of State|pages=25|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=EyAuAQAAIAAJ&pg=PA25&lpg=PA25&dq=h.+henry+powers+Morristown,+Vermont&source=bl&ots=MhtBRqf6y_&sig=Uk1ZaigYxd8sXgYIXrei6mgIyhU&hl=en&sa=X&ei=HgvWUK6QLKPi2gWm0YDIBQ&ved=0CGgQ6AEwBw#v=onepage&q=h.%20henry%20powers%20Morristown%2C%20Vermont&f=false[/tpl]  He served as a member of the council of censors in 1869, and was a member of the State constitutional convention in 1870.[tpl]cite web|url= http://www.history50states.com/VT-Lamoille-Morristown|title =Horace Henry Powers Biography|publisher= History50States.com |accessdate= December 21, 2012[/tpl]
Powers served in the Vermont State Senate in 1872 and 1873.[tpl]cite book|last=Carleton|first=Hiram|title=Genealogical and Family History of the State of Vermont: A Record of the Achievements of Her People in the Making of a Commonwealth and the Founding of a Nation|year=2003|publisher=Genealogical Publishing Com,|pages=692|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=Pi1jh7imQIAC&pg=PA692&lpg=PA692&dq=h.+henry+powers+Vermont+State+House+of+Representatives&source=bl&ots=YEfl9oUVPv&sig=3GC_xpRvl5vvxj5LEJeODJnSHqY&hl=en&sa=X&ei=FgzWUKiUK7K-2AX274GYDg&ved=0CGMQ6AEwBw#v=onepage&q=h.%20henry%20powers%20Vermont%20State%20House%20of%20Representatives&f=false[/tpl]  In 1874, he was again a member of the Vermont State House and served as Speaker.[tpl]cite book|last=Vermont General Assembly|first=and House of Representatives|title=Journal of the House of the State of Vermont|year=1877|publisher=House of Representatives|pages=605|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=OahMAAAAMAAJ&pg=PA605&lpg=PA605&dq=h.+henry+powers+Vermont+State+House+of+Representatives&source=bl&ots=_Mh2iMMvax&sig=u__V6YqUzu1x1P199bjir4RK288&hl=en&sa=X&ei=FgzWUKiUK7K-2AX274GYDg&ved=0CGYQ6AEwCA#v=onepage&q=h.%20henry%20powers%20Vermont%20State%20House%20of%20Representatives&f=false[/tpl]
Powers served as Judge of the Vermont Supreme Court from December 1874 to December 1890.[tpl]cite web|url= http://politicalgraveyard.com/bio/powers.html|title =Powers, Horace Henry (1835–1913)|publisher= The Political Graveyard|accessdate= December 21, 2012[/tpl] He was a Trustee of the University of Vermont from 1883 until his death in 1913.[tpl]cite book|last=Brown|first=John Howard|title=Lamb's biographical dictionary of the United States, Volume 6|year=1903|publisher=James H. Lamb Company|pages=331|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=4SZAAAAAYAAJ&pg=PA331&lpg=PA331&dq=horace+henry+powers+graduate+university+of+vermont&source=bl&ots=uZ2ZwtbfO1&sig=O1U8BXAsAzJc9Ungjphzw4xh0aM&hl=en&sa=X&ei=cA_WULPFGujk2QXkpYDwCw&ved=0CHAQ6AEwCA#v=onepage&q=horace%20henry%20powers%20graduate%20university%20of%20vermont&f=false[/tpl]
Powers served as a member of the Lamoille County Bank board of directors from 1888 until his death.[tpl]cite book|last=Carleton|first=Hiram|title=Genealogical and Family History of the State of Vermont: A Record of the Achievements of Her People in the Making of a Commonwealth and the Founding of a Nation|year=2003|publisher=Genealogical Publishing Com,|pages=692|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=Pi1jh7imQIAC&pg=PA692&lpg=PA692&dq=h.+henry+powers+Vermont+State+House+of+Representatives&source=bl&ots=YEfl9oUVPv&sig=3GC_xpRvl5vvxj5LEJeODJnSHqY&hl=en&sa=X&ei=FgzWUKiUK7K-2AX274GYDg&ved=0CGMQ6AEwBw#v=onepage&q=h.%20henry%20powers%20Vermont%20State%20House%20of%20Representatives&f=false[/tpl] He was a Delegate to the 1892 Republican National Convention.
Powers was elected as a Republican to the Fifty-second United States Congress and to the four succeeding Congresses, serving from March 4, 1891 until March 3, 1901.[tpl]cite web|url= http://www.govtrack.us/congress/members/horace_powers/408875|title =   Rep. Horace Powers|publisher= Govtrack.us|accessdate= December 21, 2012[/tpl] He served as chairman of the Committee on Pacific Railroads from the Fifty-fourth through Fifty-sixth Congresses.[tpl]cite web|url=http://bioguide.congress.gov/scripts/biodisplay.pl?index=P000490|title=POWERS, Horace Henry, (1835–1913)|publisher= Biographical Directory of the United States Congress|accessdate= December 21, 2012[/tpl] In 1896 he sponsored a controversial funding bill that would have allowed the Central Pacific Railroad to avoid paying of a thirty-year-old debt to the government. The bill inspired a campaign of opposition led by publisher William Randolph Hearst and his employee, Ambrose Bierce.
Powers was an unsuccessful candidate for renomination in 1900. After leaving Congress, he resumed the practice of law in Morrisville, Vermont, and was the chief counsel for the Rutland Railroad.

==Personal life==

Powers married Caroline Waterman on October 11, 1858.[tpl]cite web|url= http://www.thistledowninn.com/history.html|title =Thistledown Inn|publisher= Thistledown Inn |accessdate= December 21, 2012[/tpl] They had two children, Carrie L. Powers and George M. Powers. George M. Powers also served as the prosecuting attorney of Lamoille County, Vermont.[tpl]cite book|last=Carleton|first=Hiram|title=Genealogical and Family History of the State of Vermont: A Record of the Achievements of Her People in the Making of a Commonwealth and the Founding of a Nation|year=2003|publisher=Genealogical Publishing Com,|pages=692|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=Pi1jh7imQIAC&pg=PA692&lpg=PA692&dq=h.+henry+powers+Vermont+State+House+of+Representatives&source=bl&ots=YEfl9oUVPv&sig=3GC_xpRvl5vvxj5LEJeODJnSHqY&hl=en&sa=X&ei=FgzWUKiUK7K-2AX274GYDg&ved=0CGMQ6AEwBw#v=onepage&q=h.%20henry%20powers%20Vermont%20State%20House%20of%20Representatives&f=false[/tpl]

==Death and legacy==

He died in Morristown on December 8, 1913, and is interred in Pleasant View Cemetery.[tpl]cite web|url= http://www.findagrave.com/cgi-bin/fg.cgi?page=gr&GRid=20064844|title = Horace Henry Powers|publisher= Find A Grave|accessdate= December 21, 2012[/tpl] 
Powers home in Morrisville, Vermont is on the National Register of Historic Places by the United States Department of the Interior. The home was built around 1850 and is named the Horace Henry Powers House.[tpl]cite web|url= http://www.thistledowninn.com/history.html|title = Thistledown Inn|publisher= Thistledown Inn|accessdate= December 21, 2012[/tpl]
