[[Ray Atkeson]]

CATEGORIES: Photographers from Oregon, 1907 births, 1990 deaths, Artists from Portland, Oregon

Ray Atkeson (February 13, 1907 – May 25, 1990) was a U.S. photographer best known for his landscape images, particularly of the American West.
His best known photographs are black and white prints, many still popular in galleries, stores, books, traveling art exhibitions, and screensavers.  His awards include:

==History==

Ray Atkeson was a commercial photographer in Portland, Oregon for seventeen years 1929-1946 after arriving in Oregon in 1927.  From 1946-1973 he was a free lancer photographer and published several photo compilations.  The photobooks are mostly of Oregon and Washington, but later work included California.
His photographs captured 1930s fishing vessels recently adapted to use enginesOregon History Project to women building warships for World War II.Oregon History Project
Alan EngenAlan Engen is the son of ski champion Alf Engen; Alan is also Chairman Emeritus for the Alf Engen Ski Museum Board of Trustees; University of Utah Ski Team 1958–1962 co-captain; second place 1960 National Alpine Championship; third place 1960 NCAA ski championship called Atkeson "the finest ski photographer ever."
Atkeson lugged around heavy 4x5 camera equipment and photographed a rich variety of winter mountain scenery and activity.  Many of his alpine photos were taken before the chairlift was invented and required ski stamina.
Among his subjects were Errol Flynn, Yosemite, many of the Oregon and Washington Cascade mountains. In his later years his granddaughter Karen Schmeer assisted him with his work, including driving him to photo-shoot locations after his eyesight began to fail.[tpl]cite news|url=http://www.oregonlive.com/portland/index.ssf/2010/01/portland_native_karen_schmeer_1.html|title=Portland native Karen Schmeer, 39, remembered as loyal friend, talented film editor|last=Navas|first=Melissa|date=January 31, 2010|work=The Oregonian|accessdate=1 February 2010[/tpl]
Atkeson maintained two homes: one on the Oregon Coast, the other in Portland.
In 1988, Atkeson was one of the chief petitioners for the successful Measure 7 which added [tpl]convert|500|mi|km[/tpl] to the Oregon Scenic Waterway System originally formed by an initiative passed in 1970.
