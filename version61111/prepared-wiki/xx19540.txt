</ref> Lysenko was removed from his post as director of the Institute of Genetics at the Academy of Sciences and restricted to an experimental farm in [[Moscow]]'s [[Lenin Hills]] (the Institute itself was soon dissolved). After Khrushchev's dismissal in 1964, the president of the Academy of Sciences declared that Lysenko's immunity to criticism had officially ended. An expert commission was sent to investigate records kept at Lysenko's experimental farm. His secretive methods and ideas were revealed. A few months later, a devastating critique of Lysenko was made public.<ref>[tpl]cite book|last=Joravsky|first=David|title=The Lysenko Affair|year=1986|publisher=University of Chicago Press|location=Chicago|isbn=0-226-41032-3|page=184|url=http://books.google.co.in/books?id=nWVBgEyiiMYC&printsec[/tpl]</ref> As a result, Lysenko was immediately disgraced in the Soviet Union.<ref>[tpl]cite web|title=Trofim Denisovich Lysenko Facts|url=http://biography.yourdictionary.com/trofim-denisovich-lysenko|work=yourdictionary.com|publisher=LoveToKnow, Corp|accessdate=20 April 2014[/tpl]</ref>
Lysenko died in Moscow in 1976, and was interred in the Kuntsevo Cemetery.[tpl]cite web|title=Trofim Denisovich Lysenko|url=http://www.findagrave.com/cgi-bin/fg.cgi?page=gr&GRid=112247030|work=Find A Grave|accessdate=20 April 2014[/tpl]
