[[Voiceless alveolar stop]]

CATEGORIES: Alveolar consonants

The voiceless alveolar stop is a type of consonantal sound used in many spoken languages. The symbol in the International Phonetic Alphabet that represents voiceless dental, alveolar, and postalveolar stops is [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]IPA|t[/tpl][/tpl], and the equivalent X-SAMPA symbol is t. The dental stop can be distinguished with the underbridge diacritic, [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]IPA|t̪[/tpl][/tpl], the postalveolar with a retraction line, [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]IPA|t̠[/tpl][/tpl], and the Extensions to the IPA have a double underline diacritic which can be used to explicitly specify an alveolar pronunciation, [tpl]angle bracket|[tpl]IPA|t͇[/tpl][/tpl].
The [tpl]IPA|t[/tpl] sound is a very common sound cross-linguistically;[tpl]Harvcoltxt|Liberman|Cooper|Shankweiler|Studdert-Kennedy|1967|p=?[/tpl] the most common consonant phonemes of the world's languages are [tpl]IPA|t[/tpl], [tpl]IPA|k[/tpl] and [tpl]IPA|p[/tpl]. Most languages have at least a plain [tpl]IPA|t[/tpl], and some distinguish more than one variety. Some languages without a [tpl]IPA|t[/tpl] are Hawaiian (outside of Ni‘ihau; Hawaiian uses a voiceless velar stop when adopting loanwords with [tpl]IPA|t[/tpl]), colloquial Samoan (which also lacks an [tpl]IPA|n[/tpl]), and Nǁng of South Africa.[tpl]Citation needed|date=September 2007[/tpl]
