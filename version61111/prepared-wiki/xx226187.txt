[[Passive electioneering]]

CATEGORIES: Elections

Passive electioneering is the act of wearing campaign paraphernalia or carrying signs to a polling place with the intent of influencing voters. Across the United States laws vary relating to passive electioneering.What voters wear: A battle over dress codes, The Politico. In the fall of 2008, officials in Virginia moved to ban the wearing of campaign paraphernalia. New York has a similar law in place.
In 2008, internet political organizers are cautioning voters not to wear campaign t-shirts at the polls.Passive Electioneering, from Snopes. Organizers calculate that these obscure laws will be used to turn voters away from the polls.
At least six states, Maine, Montana, New York, Vermont, Virginia, and Kansas prohibit wearing campaign buttons, stickers and badges inside polling places.[tpl]cite web |url=http://www.sfgate.com/cgi-bin/article/article?f=/n/a/2008/10/14/politics/p112301D92.DTL |format=printable |title=Va. officials ban campaign clothing at polls |work=The Associated Press |author=BOB LEWIS, Associated Press Writer |date=14 September 2008 |accessdate=15 October 2008[/tpl] [tpl]Dead link|date=October 2010|bot=H3llBot[/tpl] Efforts to enforce a similar ban are headed to court in Pennsylvania.[tpl]Citation needed|date=October 2008[/tpl] The American Civil Liberties Union argued that the ban violates the First Amendment's right to free speech.

==New York==

The State of New York has a rarely enforced law that prohibits wearing campaign buttons or T-shirt at a polling place. In the fall of 2008, New York State Board of Elections Spokesman Bob Brehm stated that voters at the polls who wear a campaign button or T-shirt will be asked to remove the item.[tpl]cite web |url=http://www.huffingtonpost.com/nycity-news-service/no-obama-tees-obscure-law_b_129177.html |format=printable |title=No Obama Tees! ==Virginia==

The Virginia State Board of Elections voted on Tuesday October 14, 2008, to ban clothing and hats as well as buttons and other paraphernalia that directly advocate the election or defeat of a specific candidate or issue.[tpl]cite web |url=http://www.sfgate.com/cgi-bin/article/article?f=/n/a/2008/10/14/politics/p112301D92.DTL |format=printable |title=Va. officials ban campaign clothing at polls |work=The Associated Press |author=BOB LEWIS, Associated Press Writer |date=14 September 2008 |accessdate=15 October 2008[/tpl] [tpl]Dead link|date=October 2010|bot=H3llBot[/tpl]

==Canada==

While Canadian electoral law allows representatives of the political parties to be present in polling stations, nobody present at the polling station is permitted to wear or carry anything identifying themselves as a member of a political party or supporter or opponent of any political cause [tpl]Citation needed|date=October 2013[/tpl]. This includes voters [tpl]Citation needed|date=October 2013[/tpl]. Partisan officials are also not permitted to campaign immediately in front of a polling station, nor may anyone bring any campaign literature into the station [tpl]Citation needed|date=October 2013[/tpl].
The equipment used by polling staff has to meet strict colour criteria. Staff may be refused work if their clothing is of a "partisan colour" [tpl]Citation needed|date=October 2013[/tpl].
