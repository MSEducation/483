[[Geocarpy]]

CATEGORIES: Botany

Geocarpy is "an extremely rare means of plant reproduction",[tpl]cite book |author=J. D. Skinner & Christian T. Chimimba |year=2005 |title=The Mammals of the Southern African Sub-region |publisher=Cambridge University Press |isbn=9780521844185 |chapter=Order Tubulidentata |pages=35–40 |url=http://books.google.co.uk/books?id=iqwEYkTDZf4C&pg=PA39[/tpl] in which plants produce diaspores within the soil.[tpl]cite book |author=Karen van Rheede van Oudtshoorn & Margaretha W. Van Rooyen |year=1999 |title=Dispersal Biology of Desert Plants |publisher=Springer |isbn=978-3-540-64886-4 |chapter=Geocarpy |pages=117–118 |url=http://books.google.co.uk/books?id=bdTfBfsl-DcC&pg=PA117[/tpl] This may occur with subterranean flowers (protogeocarpy), or from aerial flowers, which penetrate the soil after flowering (hysterocarpy). It has evolved as an effective means of ensuring a suitable environment for the plant's offspring.
Geocarpy is most frequent in tropical or semi-desert areas, and geocarpic species may be found in the families Araceae, Begoniaceae, Brassicaceae (Cruciferae), Callitrichaceae, Convolvulaceae, Cucurbitaceae, Fabaceae (Leguminosae), Loganiaceae, Moraceae and Rubiaceae.[tpl]cite book |editor=Renate Schmidt & Ian Bancroft |year=2010 |title=Genetics and Genomics of the Brassicaceae |volume=9 |series=Plant Genetics and Genomics |publisher=Springer |isbn=9781441971180 |chapter=Brassicaceae in agriculture |author=Suzanne I. Warwick |pages=33–66 |url=http://books.google.co.uk/books?id=tMYiyo0A4XAC&pg=PA42[/tpl][tpl]cite journal |author=Alex V. Popovkin, Katherine G. Mathews, José Carlos Mendes Santos, M. Carmen Molina & Lena Struwe |year=2011 |title=Spigelia genuflexa (Loganiaceae), a new geocarpic species from the Atlantic forest of northeastern Bahia, Brazil |journal=PhytoKeys |volume=6 |pages=47–65 |doi=10.3897/phytokeys.6.1654[/tpl] The best-known example is the peanut, Arachis hypogaea.
