Jamie Whyte is the leader of ACT New Zealand, and became leader on 1 March 2014.[tpl]cite news | url= http://www.nzherald.co.nz/election-2014/news/article.cfm?c_id=1503581&objectid=11195810 | title=Act leader set to play it straight | newspaper=The New Zealand Herald | date=3 February 2014 | accessdate=3 February 2014 | author=Young, Audrey[/tpl] He is not a member of New Zealand’s parliament. ACT now has one member of parliament, John Banks, who will be standing down at the next general election, which will be held in late 2014. 

==Career==

Whyte has worked as a management consultant, as a foreign currency trader and as a philosophy lecturer at Cambridge University.Philosophy Now writes that "Whyte has published a number of papers and books on the subject of Truth, and is a one-time winner of Analysis journal’s prize for the best paper by a philosopher under the age of thirty." from Now (link to article), accessed March 2014. His philosophical publications in academic journals mainly concern the nature of truth and belief. [tpl]cite news | url= http://www.radiolive.co.nz/VIDEO-New-ACT-leader-Jamie-Whyte-interview-with-Wallace-Chapman/tabid/504/articleID/40532/Default.aspx | title=New ACT leader Jamie Whyte interview with Wallace Chapman | date=11 February 2014 | accessdate=11 February 2014 [/tpl] In 1991 he won the Analysis prize for the best article by a philosopher under the age of 30.[tpl]Citation needed|date=February 2014[/tpl]
Since 2004, Whyte has been writing for general audiences. He is the author of Crimes Against Logic (McGraw Hill, Chicago, 2004), A Load of Blair (Corvo, London, 2005), Free Thoughts (ASI, London, 2012) and Quack Policy (IEA, London, 2013).[tpl]Citation needed|date=February 2014[/tpl] He has written many columns, mainly for The Times, City AM, Standpoint, The Financial Times and the Wall Street Journal.[tpl]Citation needed|date=February 2014[/tpl] In 2006 he won the Bastiat Prize for journalism (jointly with Tim Harford of the Financial Times) and in 2010 he was runner up.[tpl]Citation needed|date=February 2014[/tpl]
Whyte’s books and articles typically attempt to expose shoddy reasoning, especially by politicians.[tpl]Citation needed|date=February 2014[/tpl] His political philosophy is classical liberalism, in the tradition of Friedrich Hayek.[tpl]Citation needed|date=March 2014[/tpl]
On 2 February 2014, he beat former MP John Boscawen for the leadership of the ACT Party.[tpl]cite web |url= http://www.3news.co.nz/ACT-choices-huge-risk-for-party/tabid/1607/articleID/330702/Default.aspx|title=ACT choices huge risk for party|author=Sabin, Brooke |date= 2 February 2014| publisher=3 News[/tpl]

===Personal Life===

Whyte was born, raised and educated to BA level in Auckland, New Zealand.[tpl]Citation needed|date=February 2014[/tpl] He then moved to the UK to study for an M.Phil and Ph.D at St John’s College, Cambridge University. Upon completion, he remained for three years as a research fellow at Corpus Christi College and a temporary lecturer in the Philosophy faculty. He then left academia and took up a job with the consultancy Oliver Wyman.[tpl]cite web |url= http://www.lse.ac.uk/publicEvents/events/2011/20110726t1830vOT.aspx|title=Keynes v Hayek |date= 8 August 2011| publisher=London School of Economics[/tpl]
